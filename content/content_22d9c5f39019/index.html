Unidentified Flying Object (UFO) ðŸ”—

An unidentified flying object (UFO), also known as unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) or unidentified anomalous phenomena (UAP), is defined as an object or phenomenon observed in the sky that cannot be readily identified or explained. While reports of unusual aerial sightings date back centuries, the term “UFO” gained prominence after World War II, particularly during the Space Age. Investigations into UFO reports have been conducted by governments, organizations, and individuals, but no definitive confirmation of claims suggesting extraterrestrial origins or other unconventional explanations has emerged. Skeptics often attribute UFO sightings to misidentified natural phenomena, human technology, delusions, or hoaxes.

Terminology ðŸ”—

The terms “flying saucers” or “flying discs” were initially used to describe UFOs, stemming from the Kenneth Arnold incident. Captain Edward J. Ruppelt of the USAF coined the acronym “UFO” to encompass a broader range of reported shapes and performances. “Unidentified aerial phenomena” (UAP) emerged later, gaining traction in the 21st century due to negative connotations associated with “UFO.” The term “extra-terrestrial vehicle” (ETV) is sometimes used to distinguish between extraterrestrial and earthbound explanations for UFOs.

Identification ðŸ”—

Most UFOs, upon investigation, are identified as ordinary objects or phenomena. Common sources of UFO reports include aircraft, astronomical objects, balloons, hoaxes, light phenomena, other atmospheric objects and phenomena, and psychological effects. Studies have shown that misidentification of prosaic phenomena, often due to inexperience or misperception, is a primary factor in UFO sightings. Astronomer Andrew Fraknoi encourages critical thinking when evaluating UFO claims, suggesting that thorough investigation often reveals less mystery than initially perceived.

History ðŸ”—

Early history before the 20th century ðŸ”—

Throughout history, people have observed unusual sights in the sky, including comets, meteors, planets, and atmospheric optical phenomena. These events were often interpreted as supernatural portents or religious omens. While some UFO enthusiasts draw parallels between religious symbols in medieval paintings and UFO reports, art historians typically offer conventional religious interpretations.

Examples of pre-contemporary reports include:

  • Julius Obsequens’ Liber de prodigiis, which describes objects moving through the sky in ancient Rome.
  • Shen Kuo’s Dream Pool Essays, which recounts eyewitness testimony of a flying object with opening doors and blinding light in 11th-century China.
  • Woodcuts from the 16th century depicting celestial phenomena over Nuremberg and Basel, which some connect to ancient astronaut claims.
  • A report in the Denison Daily News in 1878, where a farmer described seeing a large, dark, circular object resembling a saucer.

20th century and after ðŸ”—

During World War II, Allied and Axis pilots reported seeing round, glowing fireballs known as “foo fighters.” In 1946, the Swedish military collected over 2,000 reports of unidentified aerial objects, referred to as “ghost rockets.”

The modern UFO phenomenon gained momentum with the media frenzy surrounding Kenneth Arnold’s 1947 sighting near Mount Rainier. Arnold described seeing “flying saucers,” sparking widespread public interest. Weeks later, the Roswell incident occurred, involving the recovery of debris from a downed observation balloon.

In the 1950s, “saucer clubs” emerged, and books like Donald Keyhoe’s The Flying Saucers Are Real popularized the extraterrestrial hypothesis. Media speculation intensified, particularly during the 1952 UFO scare in Washington, D.C.

From 1947, the U.S. Air Force investigated UFO reports through Project Sign, Project Grudge, and Project Blue Book. Project Blue Book cataloged 12,618 sightings. The Condon Report in 1968 concluded that further UFO studies were not justified.

From the 1960s to 1990s, UFOs became integrated into popular culture, with alien abduction accounts like the Barney and Betty Hill incident gaining prominence. Films like Close Encounters of the Third Kind and Alien, along with TV series like In Search of… and The X-Files, fueled public fascination.

In December 2017, media attention resurfaced with the revelation of the secret Advanced Aviation Threat Identification Program. Leaked Pentagon UFO videos led to increased mainstream interest. In July 2021, Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb announced the Galileo Project, aiming to seek evidence of extraterrestrial artifacts. Government programs like NASA’s UAP independent study team and the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office were established to investigate UFO claims. Congressional hearings were held in 2022 and 2023, featuring whistleblower claims from former U.S. Air Force officer David Grusch.

Public belief in UFOs varies, with polls indicating that a significant percentage of Americans believe UFOs are evidence of alien life.

Notable cases and incidents ðŸ”—

Notable cases and incidents include:

  • Britain: The Rendlesham Forest incident (1980).
  • France: The Valensole UFO incident (1965) and the Trans-en-Provence Case (1981).
  • United States: The Kecksburg UFO incident (1965), the Travis Walton abduction claim (1975), and the Phoenix Lights (1997).

Famous hoaxes include the Maury Island incident and the Gulf Breeze UFO incident.

USAAF and FBI response to the 1947 sightings ðŸ”—

Following a surge in sightings in 1947, the USAAF and FBI began a formal investigation. A preliminary defense estimate concluded that “something is really flying around.”

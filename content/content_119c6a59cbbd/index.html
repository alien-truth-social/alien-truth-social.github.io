Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI) ðŸ”—

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI) encompasses various scientific projects aimed at detecting signals or evidence of intelligent life beyond Earth. These efforts involve monitoring electromagnetic radiation, searching for optical signals, and investigating potential extraterrestrial artifacts. Some initiatives also involve sending messages to hypothetical alien civilizations.

History ðŸ”—

This section covers the historical progression of SETI, from early speculations to modern scientific endeavors.

Early Work ðŸ”—

Early searches for extraterrestrial intelligence within the Solar System are highlighted. Nikola Tesla suggested using his wireless electrical transmission system to contact beings on Mars in 1896. Guglielmo Marconi, Lord Kelvin, and David Peck Todd also believed radio could be used to contact Martians. In 1924, a “National Radio Silence Day” was promoted in the United States to listen for Martian signals during a close opposition of Mars.

Sentinel, META, and BETA ðŸ”—

This section details the development of spectrum analyzers for SETI. Harvard University physicist Paul Horowitz designed spectrum analyzers specifically for SETI transmissions. Project Sentinel, using a portable spectrum analyzer, began in 1983 at Oak Ridge Observatory. It was followed by Project META (Megachannel Extra-Terrestrial Assay) in 1985, which had a capacity of 8.4 million channels. META II was started in Argentina in 1990 to search the southern sky. The follow-on to META was named BETA (Billion-channel Extraterrestrial Assay), which commenced observation in 1995.

MOP and Project Phoenix ðŸ”—

This section discusses the NASA Microwave Observing Program (MOP), which was canceled one year after its start due to opposition in the United States Congress. The SETI Institute resurrected the MOP program under the name of Project Phoenix in 1995, backed by private funding. Project Phoenix, directed by Jill Tarter, studied roughly 1,000 nearby Sun-like stars until approximately 2015.

Ongoing Radio Searches ðŸ”—

This section focuses on current radio SETI searches. Many radio frequencies penetrate Earth’s atmosphere well, leading to the use of radio telescopes. Earth has been sending radio waves into space for over 100 years, reaching over 1,000 stars. Many international radio telescopes are currently being used for radio SETI searches, including the Low Frequency Array (LOFAR) in Europe, the Murchison Widefield Array (MWA) in Australia, and the Lovell Telescope in the United Kingdom.

Allen Telescope Array ðŸ”—

The SETI Institute collaborated with the Radio Astronomy Laboratory at the Berkeley SETI Research Center to develop the Allen Telescope Array (ATA). The array has 42 operational dishes at the Hat Creek Radio Observatory in rural northern California. The SETI Institute’s Center for SETI Research (CSR) uses ATA in the search for extraterrestrial intelligence, observing 12 hours a day, 7 days a week.

SERENDIP ðŸ”—

SERENDIP (Search for Extraterrestrial Radio Emissions from Nearby Developed Intelligent Populations) is a SETI program launched in 1979 by the Berkeley SETI Research Center. SERENDIP analyzes deep space radio telescope data obtained while other astronomers are using the telescopes.

Breakthrough Listen ðŸ”—

Breakthrough Listen is a ten-year initiative with $100 million funding begun in July 2015 to actively search for intelligent extraterrestrial communications in the universe. The science program for Breakthrough Listen is based at Berkeley SETI Research Center. The project is observing for thousands of hours every year on two major radio telescopes, the Green Bank Observatory in West Virginia, and the Parkes Observatory in Australia.

FAST ðŸ”—

China’s 500 meter Aperture Spherical Telescope (FAST) lists detecting interstellar communication signals as part of its science mission. FAST is the first radio observatory built with SETI as a core scientific goal. In June 2022, astronomers working with FAST reported the possibility of having detected artificial signals, but cautioned that further studies were required.

UCLA ðŸ”—

Since 2016, University of California Los Angeles (UCLA) students have been participating in radio searches for technosignatures with the Green Bank Telescope. Targets include the Kepler field, TRAPPIST-1, and solar-type stars.

Community SETI Projects ðŸ”—

This section highlights SETI projects that rely on community participation.

SETI@home ðŸ”—

The SETI@home project used volunteer computing to analyze signals acquired by the SERENDIP project. The project was launched by the Berkeley SETI Research Center at the University of California, Berkeley, in May 1999. On March 31, 2020, the project stopped sending out new work to SETI@home users.

SETI Net ðŸ”—

SETI Network was the only fully operational private search system. The SETI Net station consisted of off-the-shelf, consumer-grade electronics to minimize cost. SETI Net started operation in the early 1980s and was decommissioned on 2021-12-04.

The SETI League and Project Argus ðŸ”—

Founded in 1994 in response to the United States Congress cancellation of the NASA SETI program, The SETI League, Incorporated is a membership-supported nonprofit organization. The SETI League pioneered the conversion of backyard satellite TV dishes into research-grade radio telescopes. The organization concentrates on coordinating a global network of small, amateur-built radio telescopes under Project Argus.

Optical Experiments ðŸ”—

This section discusses the possibility that alien civilizations might be using powerful lasers for interstellar communications at optical wavelengths. Several optical SETI experiments are now in progress. A Harvard-Smithsonian group designed a laser detector and mounted it on Harvard’s 155-centimeter optical telescope. The University of California, Berkeley, is also conducting optical SETI searches and collaborates with the NIROSETI program. The SETI Institute also runs a program called ‘Laser SETI’.

Quantum Communications ðŸ”—

This section explores the possibility of using quantum communications for interstellar communication. A 2021 preprint described how one could search for quantum communication transmissions sent by ETI using existing telescope and receiver technology.

Search for Extraterrestrial Artifacts ðŸ”—

This section discusses the possibility of using interstellar messenger probes in the search for extraterrestrial intelligence. In 1996, ‘Invitation to ETI’ was established by Allen Tough, as a Web-based SETI experiment inviting spacefaring probes to establish contact with humanity. In 1979, Freitas and Valdes conducted a photographic search of the vicinity of the Earth-Moon triangular libration points L4 and L5, seeking possible orbiting extraterrestrial interstellar probes, but found nothing.

Technosignatures ðŸ”—

Technosignatures, including all signs of technology, are a recent avenue in the search for extraterrestrial intelligence. Technosignatures may originate from various sources, from megastructures such as Dyson spheres to the atmospheric contamination created by an industrial civilization.

Advances for Bio and Technosignature Detection ðŸ”—

This section highlights the development of an algorithm for signal reconstruction in zero-knowledge one-way communication channels. Another method called fractal messaging offers a framework for space-time scale-free communication.

Fermi Paradox ðŸ”—

This section addresses the Fermi paradox, which questions why extraterrestrials have not visited Earth or why signals from extraterrestrials have not been heard. There are multiple explanations proposed for the Fermi paradox, ranging from analyses suggesting that intelligent life is rare to analyses suggesting that extraterrestrial civilizations may be common, but they would not communicate with us.

Difficulty of Detection ðŸ”—

A significant problem is the vastness of space. For SERENDIP and most other SETI projects to detect a signal from an extraterrestrial civilization, the civilization would have to be beaming a powerful signal directly at us.

Post-Detection Disclosure Protocol ðŸ”—

The International Academy of Astronautics (IAA) has a long-standing SETI Permanent Study Group (SPSG), which addresses matters of SETI science, technology, and international policy. Active SETI ðŸ”—

Active SETI, also known as messaging to extraterrestrial intelligence (METI), consists of sending signals into space in the hope that they will be detected by an alien intelligence.

Realized Interstellar Radio Message Projects ðŸ”—

In November 1974, an attempt was made at the Arecibo Observatory to send a message to other worlds. Known as the Arecibo Message, it was sent towards the globular cluster M13. Further IRMs Cosmic Call, Teen Age Message, Cosmic Call 2, and A Message From Earth were transmitted in 1999, 2001, 2003 and 2008 from the Evpatoria Planetary Radar.

Debate ðŸ”—

Whether or not to attempt to contact extraterrestrials has attracted significant academic debate. Physicist Stephen Hawking suggests that “alerting” extraterrestrial intelligences to our existence is foolhardy. Astronomer Jill Tarter disagrees with Hawking, arguing that aliens developed and long-lived enough to communicate and travel across interstellar distances would have evolved a cooperative and less violent intelligence.

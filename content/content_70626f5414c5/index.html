This article discusses various conspiracy theories surrounding Unidentified Flying Objects (UFOs), focusing on the alleged suppression of evidence by governments, particularly the United States government, regarding extraterrestrial intelligence or advanced technology derived from alien sources. It also touches upon claims of government cooperation with extraterrestrials and the allowance of events like cattle mutilation and alien abduction.

Overview ðŸ”—

This section introduces the historical context of UFO conspiracy theories, tracing their origins to pulp science fiction and “lost continent” myths. It mentions Kenneth Arnold’s 1947 sighting and the subsequent surge in UFO reports. Key figures like Donald Keyhoe, who popularized the idea of a government cover-up, and the introduction of the “Men in Black” concept are highlighted. The section also covers the evolution of these theories, linking them to events like the Kennedy assassination and dramatizations in popular culture, such as the film Close Encounters of the Third Kind. It notes the shift from theories about benevolent “space brothers” to darker narratives involving malevolent aliens and government cabals. The section concludes by mentioning the renewed interest in UFO conspiracy theories following Leslie Kean’s reporting on a 21st-century Pentagon UFO program.

Mainstream Views ðŸ”—

This section presents the mainstream scientific perspective on UFOs, emphasizing that while unusual sightings have been reported throughout history, there is no confirmed evidence to support claims of extraterrestrial origins. It mentions government investigations like Project Blue Book and Project Condign, as well as skeptical organizations like the Committee for Skeptical Inquiry, which offer prosaic explanations for UFO sightings, such as natural phenomena, human technology, delusions, and hoaxes. The section also touches upon the psychosocial explanations for UFO beliefs, viewing them as modern folklore and mythology. Benjamin Radford’s argument about the unlikelihood of a decades-long, international government cover-up is also presented.

This subsection focuses on the role of Raymond Palmer and his Amazing Stories magazine in the emergence of UFO conspiracy theories. It mentions Palmer’s publication of reports of strange craft and his hiring of Kenneth Arnold to investigate a flying disc report. The subsection also highlights Palmer’s promotion of the idea that the truth behind the discs was being covered up.

This subsection discusses the “Shaver Mystery” stories published in Amazing Stories, based on the writings of Richard Shaver, who claimed to be in telepathic communication with a secret underground race. It notes Shaver’s history of psychiatric problems and the blurring of fact and fiction in his stories. The subsection also mentions Palmer’s argument that “responsible parties in world governments” were aware of spaceships visiting Earth.

This subsection covers the beginning of the flying disc craze with Kenneth Arnold’s sighting and the subsequent surge in UFO reports. It mentions the Roswell incident and the Army’s initial press release about recovering a “flying disc.” The subsection also discusses Palmer’s contact with Arnold and their investigation of Fred Crisman’s claims near Maury Island, Washington.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This subsection describes the aftermath of the 1947 flying disc craze, including Palmer’s argument that the flap was proof of Richard Sharpe Shaver’s claims. It quotes Shaver’s prediction that the truth behind the discs would never be disclosed to the common people. The subsection also mentions Shaver’s devotion to “rock books” and Palmer’s admission that Shaver had spent time in a mental institution. It concludes by noting Palmer’s later linking of flying saucers, the Shaver Mystery, the Kennedy assassination, Watergate, and Fred Crisman.

This section details the increased governmental and civilian investigative efforts and reports of public disinformation and suppression of evidence during the 1950s.

This subsection focuses on the first “alien bodies” conspiracy theory, which emerged in 1949 with Frank Scully’s columns in Variety. It mentions Scully’s claims that dead extraterrestrial beings were recovered from a flying saucer crash and his book Behind the Flying Saucers. The subsection also notes the later revelation that Scully had been the victim of confidence artists and the exposure of the hoax by True magazine.

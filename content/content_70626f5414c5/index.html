UFO Conspiracy Theories ðŸ”—

This Wikipedia entry delves into the realm of UFO conspiracy theories, primarily focusing on the belief that governments, particularly the United States government, are actively suppressing evidence of extraterrestrial life and technology. It also touches upon theories involving government cooperation with extraterrestrials and the allowance of events like cattle mutilation and alien abduction.

Overview ðŸ”—

This section provides a historical context for UFO conspiracy theories, tracing their origins to pulp science fiction and “lost continent” myths. It mentions Kenneth Arnold’s 1947 sighting and the subsequent surge in UFO reports. Key figures like Donald Keyhoe, who popularized the idea of a government cover-up, and the introduction of the “Men in Black” concept are discussed. The section also highlights the impact of events like the Kennedy assassination and films like Close Encounters of the Third Kind on the prevalence of these theories. It notes the shift in the 1980s towards darker theories involving malevolent aliens and government collaboration, as well as the rise of UFO conspiracy theories in 1990s popular culture. The section concludes with the renewed interest in these theories following Leslie Kean’s reporting on a Pentagon UFO program in 2017.

Mainstream Views ðŸ”—

This section presents the mainstream scientific perspective on UFOs, emphasizing that while unusual sightings have been reported throughout history, there is no solid evidence to support claims of extraterrestrial contact or government cover-ups. It mentions government investigations like Project Blue Book and Project Condign, as well as the role of skeptic organizations like the Committee for Skeptical Inquiry in providing prosaic explanations for UFO sightings. The section also touches upon the psychosocial explanations for UFO beliefs, linking them to folklore, mythology, and new religious movements. It includes Benjamin Radford’s argument against the feasibility of a long-term, global UFO cover-up.

Notable Proponents ðŸ”—

This section identifies key individuals who have promoted UFO conspiracy theories. It mentions Donald Keyhoe, Jacque Vallee, J. Allen Hynek, and Stanton Friedman. It also notes that some proponents, like Richard Shaver and Paul Bennewitz, had mental health issues, while others, like Gray Barker and Bill Moore, confessed to hoaxes or lies. The section lists figures like Barry Goldwater, Lord Hill-Norton, Roscoe H. Hillenkoetter, Gordon Cooper, and Edgar Mitchell, who have suggested that UFO evidence is being suppressed. 21st-century proponents such as Paul Hellyer, Garry Nolan, Haim Eshed, Jim Semivan, Harold E. Puthoff, Tom DeLonge, Luis Elizondo, and David Grusch are also mentioned.

In Popular Fiction ðŸ”—

This section explores the presence of government UFO cover-ups in popular culture. It cites films like 2001: A Space Odyssey, Easy Rider, Close Encounters of the Third Kind, E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial, Total Recall, Independence Day, and Men in Black, as well as TV shows like The X-Files, Dark Skies, and Stargate. It also mentions John Glenn’s appearance on Frasier and the Sidney Sheldon novel The Doomsday Conspiracy.

In Religion ðŸ”—

This section examines the intersection of UFO conspiracy theories and new religious movements. It identifies groups like the Nation of Islam, Scientology, and Heaven’s Gate as proponents of these theories. It also discusses Mormon cosmology and its connection to beliefs about inhabited planets. The section mentions figures like Meade Layne, George Adamski, George Hunt Williamson, and Guy Warren Ballard, as well as UFO religions like Unarius, the Aetherius Society, RaÃ«lism, and the Order of the Solar Temple.

1940s origin in Raymond Palmer’s pulp magazine ðŸ”—

This subsection focuses on Raymond Palmer, the editor of Amazing Stories, and his role in popularizing UFO conspiracy theories. It mentions his publication of Richard Shaver’s stories about a secret underground race and Palmer’s claim that governments were aware of spaceships visiting Earth.

Spaceships of “The Shaver Mystery” ðŸ”—

This subsection details Richard Shaver’s claims of telepathic communication with a secret underground race and their mastery of space travel. It notes Shaver’s history of psychiatric problems and the blurring of fact and fiction in his stories.

Kenneth Arnold ignites flying disc craze ðŸ”—

This subsection covers Kenneth Arnold’s 1947 sighting and the subsequent “flying disc craze.” It mentions the Roswell incident and Palmer’s investigation of Fred Crisman’s claims near Maury Island, Washington.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the aftermath of the 1947 flying disc craze, including Palmer’s argument that it proved Shaver’s claims and Shaver’s prediction that the truth about the discs would remain a secret. It also mentions Palmer’s later linking of UFOs to the JFK assassination and Watergate.

This section discusses the increase in governmental and civilian investigative efforts and reports of public disinformation and suppression of evidence.

This subsection explores the origins of the “Men in Black” legend, tracing it back to Gray Barker’s book They Knew Too Much About Flying Saucers.

This section discusses the increasing government skepticism in the 1960s and 1970s, fueled by events like the Kennedy assassination, the Pentagon Papers, and the Watergate scandal.

This subsection examines Jim Garrison’s prosecution of Clay Shaw and the subpoena of Fred Crisman, linking Crisman to the Kennedy assassination and a supposed UFO cover-up.

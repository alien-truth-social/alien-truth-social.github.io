UFO Conspiracy Theories ðŸ”—

This Wikipedia entry delves into the world of UFO conspiracy theories, exploring the belief that governments, particularly the United States government, are suppressing evidence of extraterrestrial life and technology. It examines the historical development of these theories, their proponents, and their presence in popular culture and religion.

Overview ðŸ”—

This section introduces the core concept of UFO conspiracy theories: the alleged suppression of evidence regarding extraterrestrial-controlled UFOs or technology. It notes the evolution of these theories, from initial claims of government cover-ups to more complex narratives involving communication and cooperation with extraterrestrials, including claims of government allowance of cattle mutilation and alien abduction. It also mentions the Committee for Skeptical Inquiry’s stance that there is little to no evidence supporting these theories.

Mainstream Views ðŸ”—

This section presents the mainstream scientific perspective on UFOs. It acknowledges the historical and cultural prominence of UFO sightings, particularly after World War II and during the Space Age. It highlights government and independent investigations that have failed to confirm extraordinary claims made by ufologists. Prosaic explanations for UFO sightings, such as natural phenomena, human technology, delusions, and hoaxes, are offered. The section also touches upon the psychosocial explanations for UFO beliefs and their influence on new religions. Benjamin Radford’s argument about the unlikelihood of a decades-long, international cover-up is also presented.

Notable Proponents ðŸ”—

This section identifies key figures associated with UFO conspiracy theories. Donald Keyhoe, Jacque Vallee, J. Allen Hynek, and Stanton Friedman are mentioned as prominent voices from the 1950s to the 1970s. It also notes that some early proponents, like Richard Shaver and Paul Bennewitz, suffered from psychiatric issues, and that some proponents confessed to hoaxes or lies. The section lists individuals in positions of authority, such as Senator Barry Goldwater, Admiral Lord Hill-Norton, Vice Admiral Roscoe H. Hillenkoetter, and astronauts Gordon Cooper and Edgar Mitchell, who have suggested that UFO evidence is being suppressed. 21st-century proponents like Paul Hellyer, Garry Nolan, Haim Eshed, Jim Semivan, Harold E. Puthoff, Tom DeLonge, Luis Elizondo, and David Grusch are also mentioned.

In Popular Fiction ðŸ”—

This section explores the portrayal of government cover-ups of extraterrestrial contact in popular fiction. Films like 2001: A Space Odyssey, Easy Rider, Close Encounters of the Third Kind, E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial, Flight of the Navigator, Total Recall, Independence Day, and Men in Black are cited as examples. Television series such as The X-Files, Dark Skies, and Stargate are also mentioned. The section also notes John Glenn’s appearance on Frasier, Sidney Sheldon’s novel The Doomsday Conspiracy, and the series American Horror Story: Double Feature.

In Religion ðŸ”—

This section examines the intersection of UFO conspiracy theories and religion. It identifies new religious movements, including the Nation of Islam, Scientology, and Heaven’s Gate, that have incorporated UFO-related beliefs. Mormon cosmology, Theosophy, and the “I AM” Activity are also discussed. The section mentions the Nation of Islam’s concept of “The Mother Plane,” the UFO religions Unarius and the Aetherius Society, L. Ron Hubbard’s teachings about Xenu in Scientology, and the RaÃ«lism movement. It concludes with the Heaven’s Gate mass suicide and the Order of the Solar Temple mass deaths.

Chronology of UFO Conspiracy Theories ðŸ”—

This section provides a detailed timeline of the development of UFO conspiracy theories, starting with Raymond Palmer’s pulp magazine Amazing Stories in the 1940s.

1940s Origin in Raymond Palmer’s Pulp Magazine ðŸ”—

This subsection focuses on Raymond Palmer, the editor of Amazing Stories, and his role in popularizing UFO conspiracy theories. It mentions his publication of Richard Shaver’s stories about a secret underground race and Palmer’s claim that world governments were aware of spaceships visiting Earth.

Spaceships of “The Shaver Mystery” ðŸ”—

This subsection details Richard Shaver’s claims of telepathic communication with a secret underground race and their mastery of space travel. It notes Shaver’s history of psychiatric problems and the blurring of fact and fiction in his stories.

Kenneth Arnold Ignites Flying Disc Craze ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the beginning of the flying disc craze with Kenneth Arnold’s sighting in 1947. It mentions the Roswell incident and the Army’s initial press release about recovering a “flying disc.” It also discusses Palmer’s investigation of Fred Crisman’s claims near Maury Island, Washington, and the subsequent plane crash.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This subsection describes the aftermath of the Maury Island incident and the continued promotion of UFO conspiracy theories by Palmer and Shaver. It mentions Shaver’s “rock books” and Palmer’s later admission that Shaver had spent time in a mental institution. It also notes Palmer’s claim of a link between flying saucers, the Shaver Mystery, the Kennedy assassination, Watergate, and Fred Crisman.

National Coverage in the 1950s ðŸ”—

This section discusses the increase in governmental and civilian investigative efforts and reports of public disinformation and suppression of evidence in the 1950s.

The Flying Saucer Conspiracy ðŸ”—

This subsection focuses on Donald Keyhoe’s role in promoting UFO conspiracy theories in the 1950s. It mentions his article “The Flying Saucers Are Real” and his book The Flying Saucer Conspiracy, which accused the government of covering up knowledge of flying saucers. It also describes Keyhoe’s appearance on CBS’s Armstrong Circle Theatre and the cutting of his microphone during the live broadcast.

Aztec “Alien Bodies” Hoax ðŸ”—

This subsection details the Frank Scully’s “alien bodies” conspiracy theory, which claimed that dead extraterrestrial beings were recovered from a flying saucer crash. It mentions Scully’s book Behind the Flying Saucers and the later exposure of Scully as a victim of confidence artists.

Air Force Accounts ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the Air Force’s response to UFO sightings in the 1950s. It mentions General John Samford’s press conference on the Washington D.C. UFO incident, the Robertson Panel’s recommendation for public education, and Al Chop’s role as the press spokesman for Project Bluebook. It also discusses Captain Edward J. Ruppelt’s book The Report on Unidentified Flying Objects.

“Philadelphia Experiment” Hoax ðŸ”—

This subsection describes the “Philadelphia Experiment” hoax, which involved claims of a navy ship being made invisible and teleporting. It mentions Morris K. Jessup’s book The Case for the UFO and the letters he received from “Carlos Miguel Allende” (Carl Meredith Allen). It also discusses the annotated copy of Jessup’s book and Jessup’s death by suicide.

‘Men in Black’ Legends ðŸ”—

This subsection explores the legend of the Men in Black, popularized by Gray Barker’s book They Knew Too Much About Flying Saucers. It mentions the speculation that the men in black are government agents who harass and threaten UFO witnesses.

1960-70s: Growing Distrust in Government ðŸ”—

This section discusses the growing government skepticism in the 1960s and 1970s, fueled by events like the Kennedy assassination, the Pentagon Papers, and the Watergate scandal.

Michigan “Swamp Gas” UFO Reports ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the 1966 UFO reports in Michigan and the Air Force’s explanation that they were caused by swamp gas. It notes the ridicule that followed and the call for a Congressional investigation.

Fred Crisman and JFK Conspiracy Theories ðŸ”—

This subsection details Fred Crisman’s involvement in the JFK assassination conspiracy theories. It mentions Jim Garrison’s prosecution of Clay Shaw and Crisman’s testimony before the New Orleans grand jury.

“Cosmic Watergate” ðŸ”—

This subsection explores the concept of a “Cosmic Watergate,” referring to a government cover-up of UFO information. It mentions the film 2001: A Space Odyssey and J. Allen Hynek’s claim that Bluebook was a “Cosmic Watergate.”

Alien Bodies at “Hangar 18” ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the legends of alien bodies being stored at “Hangar 18” at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base. It mentions Robert Spencer Carr’s claim that alien bodies recovered from the Aztec crash were stored there.

“Alien Abductions” ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the rise of alien abduction claims in the 1970s. It mentions the Betty and Barney Hill case, the Travis Walton incident, and the work of Budd Hopkins and Whitley Strieber.

Close Encounters of the Third Kind ðŸ”—

This subsection explores the influence of the film Close Encounters of the Third Kind on UFO conspiracy theories. It mentions the film’s depiction of a government cover-up and the involvement of J. Allen Hynek and Jacques Vallee.

Jesse Marcel and Roswell ðŸ”—

This subsection details Jesse Marcel’s role in the Roswell incident and his later statements suggesting that the debris might have been extraterrestrial. It mentions the National Enquirer’s coverage of the story and the US military’s later reports disclosing the true nature of the crashed aircraft.

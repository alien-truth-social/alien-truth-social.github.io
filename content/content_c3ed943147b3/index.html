Spacecraft ðŸ”—

A spacecraft is a vehicle or machine designed to fly and operate in outer space. It serves various purposes, including communications, Earth observation, meteorology, navigation, space colonization, planetary exploration, and transporting humans and cargo. Spacecraft typically require a launch vehicle to reach space, except for single-stage-to-orbit vehicles.

Crewed and Uncrewed Spacecraft ðŸ”—

Spacecraft are categorized into crewed and uncrewed vehicles.

Crewed Spacecraft ðŸ”—

As of 2016, only Russia, the United States, and China have flown crewed spacecraft. Vostok 1, carrying Soviet cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin in 1961, was the first crewed spacecraft to complete a full Earth orbit. The second crewed spacecraft was Freedom 7, which performed a sub-orbital spaceflight in 1961 carrying American astronaut Alan Shepard. Other crewed spacecraft include the Soviet Voskhod and Soyuz, the American Gemini and Apollo spacecraft, the Skylab space station, the Space Shuttle, and the Chinese Shenzhou. The International Space Station (ISS), crewed since November 2000, is a joint venture between Russia, the United States, Canada, and other countries.

Uncrewed Spacecraft ðŸ”—

Uncrewed spacecraft operate autonomously or telerobotically, often preferred for cost-effectiveness and lower risk. They are used for missions unsuitable for humans, such as exploring Venus or the outer planets. Robotic spacecraft used for scientific research are space probes, while those orbiting a planetary body are artificial satellites. Examples include the Hubble Space Telescope and cargo spacecraft like the Jules Verne Automated Transfer Vehicle (ATV).

Other ðŸ”—

Some spacecraft, like the Buran spaceplane and SpaceX Dragon 2, can operate as both crewed and uncrewed vehicles.

Types of Spacecraft ðŸ”—

Spacecraft are designed for various specific purposes.

Communications Satellite ðŸ”—

Communications satellites relay and amplify radio telecommunication signals between a source transmitter and a receiver on Earth. They are used for television, telephone, radio, internet, and military applications. Many are in geostationary orbit, while others form satellite constellations in low Earth orbit.

Cargo Spacecraft ðŸ”—

Cargo spacecraft are robotic vehicles designed to carry cargo, such as food, propellant, and other supplies, to support space station operations. Automated cargo spacecraft have serviced Salyut 6, Salyut 7, Mir, the International Space Station, and Tiangong space station.

Space Probes ðŸ”—

Space probes are robotic spacecraft sent to explore deep space or astronomical bodies other than Earth. They have visited every planet in the Solar System and Pluto. The Voyager probes, launched in 1977, took advantage of a rare alignment of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune to visit all four planets in one mission.

Voyager Program ðŸ”—

The Voyager probes, launched in 1977, took advantage of a rare planetary alignment to visit Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 are now escaping the Solar System, providing valuable data from interstellar space.

Space Telescopes ðŸ”—

Space telescopes are telescopes in outer space used to observe astronomical objects, avoiding the filtering and distortion of electromagnetic radiation and light pollution. Examples include the Hubble Space Telescope and the James Webb Space Telescope.

Landers ðŸ”—

Landers are spacecraft that make a soft landing on the surface of an astronomical body other than Earth. They use various techniques, including fuel supply and aerobraking, to achieve a safe landing.

Space Capsules ðŸ”—

Space capsules are spacecraft that can return from space at least once. They have a blunt shape and do not possess wings. The Vostok capsule, built by the Soviet Union, carried the first person in space, Yuri Gagarin.

Spaceplanes ðŸ”—

Spaceplanes are spacecraft built in the shape of airplanes. The North American X-15 was an early example, while the Space Shuttle orbiter was the first reusable orbital spaceplane. The Buran-class shuttle, launched by the USSR, was the first autonomous reusable spaceplane.

Space Shuttle ðŸ”—

The Space Shuttle was a reusable Low Earth Orbital launch system consisting of two reusable Solid Rocket Boosters, a Space Shuttle Orbiter, and a throwaway Space Shuttle external tank. It flew 135 times from 1981 to 2011, supporting Mir, the Hubble Space Telescope, and the International Space Station.

Other ðŸ”—

Some spacecraft do not fit neatly into the general categories.

SpaceX Starship ðŸ”—

Starship is a spacecraft and second stage under development by SpaceX, designed to transport crew and cargo to Earth orbit, the Moon, Mars, and beyond. It will also be capable of point-to-point transport on Earth and refueling other Starship vehicles.

Mission Extension Vehicle ðŸ”—

The Mission Extension Vehicle is a robotic spacecraft designed to prolong the life of another spacecraft by docking to it and correcting its orientation or orbit.

Subsystems ðŸ”—

A spacecraft’s astrionics system comprises various subsystems, including attitude determination and control, guidance, navigation and control, communications, command and data handling, power, thermal control, propulsion, and structures. Spacecraft intended for human spaceflight must also include a life support system.

Spacecraft records include achievements such as speed and distance from the Sun.

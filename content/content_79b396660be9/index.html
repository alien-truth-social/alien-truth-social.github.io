UFO Conspiracy Theories ðŸ”—

This article discusses the various conspiracy theories surrounding UFOs, focusing on the idea that governments, particularly the United States government, are suppressing evidence of extraterrestrial life or technology. It also touches upon the alleged communication or cooperation between world governments and extraterrestrials, as well as claims of government involvement in cattle mutilations and alien abductions. The Committee for Skeptical Inquiry is cited as stating that there is little to no evidence to support these theories.

Overview ðŸ”—

This section provides a historical context for UFO conspiracy theories, tracing their origins to pulp science fiction and “lost continent” myths. It mentions Kenneth Arnold’s 1947 sighting and the subsequent wave of UFO reports. Donald Keyhoe is credited with popularizing the idea of a government cover-up. The section also discusses the emergence of the “Men in Black” concept and the linking of UFOs to the Kennedy assassination. It notes the rise of theories involving malevolent aliens, alien abductions, and cattle mutilations in the 1980s, as well as Bill Moore’s confession of feeding fake evidence to UFO researchers. The popularity of UFO conspiracy theories in 1990s popular culture, with shows like The X-Files, is also mentioned. The section concludes by noting the renewed interest in UFO conspiracy theories following Leslie Kean’s stories about a 21st-century Pentagon UFO program.

Mainstream Views ðŸ”—

This section presents the mainstream scientific perspective on UFOs. It acknowledges the cultural prominence of UFOs after World War II and the Space Age, and mentions government investigations like Project Blue Book and Project Condign. It highlights that these investigations have not confirmed claims of extraterrestrial intelligence or other unconventional hypotheses. The section emphasizes that scientists and skeptical organizations offer prosaic explanations for UFOs, attributing them to natural phenomena, human technology, delusions, and hoaxes. It also notes that beliefs surrounding UFOs have inspired new religions and that social scientists view the ongoing interest in UFOs as a modern example of folklore and mythology. Benjamin Radford’s argument about the unlikelihood of a decades-long, international UFO cover-up is also presented.

Notable Proponents ðŸ”—

This section identifies key figures who have promoted UFO conspiracy theories. Donald Keyhoe, Jacque Vallee, J. Allen Hynek, and Stanton Friedman are mentioned as prominent proponents from the 1950s to the 1970s. The section also notes that some proponents, like Richard Shaver and Paul Bennewitz, had mental health issues, while others, like Gray Barker and Bill Moore, confessed to hoaxes or lies. Individuals who have suggested that UFO evidence is being suppressed, including Barry Goldwater, Lord Hill-Norton, Roscoe H. Hillenkoetter, Gordon Cooper, and Edgar Mitchell, are also listed. 21st-century proponents such as Paul Hellyer, Garry Nolan, Haim Eshed, Jim Semivan, Harold E. Puthoff, Tom DeLonge, Luis Elizondo, and David Grusch are also mentioned.

In Popular Fiction ðŸ”—

This section explores the portrayal of government cover-ups of extraterrestrial contact in popular fiction. It mentions films like 2001: A Space Odyssey, Easy Rider, Close Encounters of the Third Kind, E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial, Flight of the Navigator, Total Recall, Independence Day, and Men in Black, as well as TV shows like The X-Files, Dark Skies, and Stargate. John Glenn’s appearance on Frasier, where he confesses to a UFO cover-up, is also noted. The Sidney Sheldon novel The Doomsday Conspiracy and the American Horror Story: Double Feature series are also mentioned.

Mormon cosmology’s teachings about inhabited planets are also mentioned. Theosophists and occultists, as well as “contactees” like Meade Layne, George Adamski, and George Hunt Williamson, are discussed. The section also covers the Nation of Islam’s concept of The Mother Plane, the UFO religions of the 1950s, including Unarius and the Aetherius Society, and L. Ron Hubbard’s Scientology. This section provides a detailed timeline of the development of UFO conspiracy theories, starting with Raymond Palmer’s pulp magazine in the 1940s.

1940s Origin in Raymond Palmer’s Pulp Magazine ðŸ”—

This subsection focuses on the role of Raymond Palmer and his Amazing Stories magazine in the emergence of UFO conspiracy theories. It mentions Palmer’s publication of reports of strange craft and his hiring of Kenneth Arnold to investigate a UFO report near Maury Island, Washington. The subsection also notes Palmer’s magazine’s claims of a cover-up and his later linking of UFOs to the JFK assassination and Watergate.

Spaceships of “The Shaver Mystery” ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the “Shaver Mystery,” a series of stories published in Amazing Stories based on the writings of Richard Shaver, who claimed to be in telepathic communication with a secret underground race. It mentions Shaver’s claims of ancient civilizations mastering space travel and Palmer’s argument that “responsible parties in world governments” were aware of spaceships visiting Earth.

Kenneth Arnold Ignites Flying Disc Craze ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the beginning of the flying disc craze with Kenneth Arnold’s sighting and the subsequent spread of UFO reports. It mentions the Roswell incident and the Army’s initial press release about recovering a “flying disc.” The subsection also discusses Palmer’s contact with Arnold and their investigation of Fred Crisman’s claims near Maury Island, Washington.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This subsection describes the aftermath of the flying disc craze, including Palmer’s argument that the flap was proof of Richard Sharpe Shaver’s claims. It quotes Shaver’s prediction that the truth behind the discs would remain a secret. The subsection also notes Palmer’s later admission that Shaver had spent eight years in a mental institution and Palmer’s linking of UFOs to the Kennedy assassination and Watergate.

National Coverage in the 1950s ðŸ”—

This section discusses the increase in governmental and civilian investigative efforts and reports of public disinformation and suppression of evidence in the 1950s.

Aztec “Alien Bodies” Hoax ðŸ”—

This subsection focuses on the first “alien bodies” conspiracy theory, which emerged with Frank Scully’s columns in Variety and his book Behind the Flying Saucers. It mentions Scully’s claims of dead extraterrestrial beings recovered from flying saucer crashes and the later revelation that Scully had been the victim of confidence artists.

Air Force Accounts ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the Air Force’s handling of UFO reports, including General John Samford’s press conference on the recent UFO sightings over the nation’s capital. It mentions the Robertson Panel’s recommendation of a public education campaign and the film Unidentified Flying Objects: The True Story of Flying Saucers, which dramatized the events of the early 1950s. The subsection also discusses Captain Edward J. Ruppelt’s book The Report on Unidentified Flying Objects.

“Philadelphia Experiment” Hoax ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the “Philadelphia Experiment” hoax, which involved claims of a navy ship being made invisible and teleporting from Philadelphia to Norfolk, Virginia. It mentions Morris K. Jessup’s book The Case for the UFO and the letters he received from “Carlos Miguel Allende” (Carl Meredith Allen). The subsection also covers Jessup’s death by suicide and the promotion of the Philadelphia Experiment by Charles Berlitz.

‘Men in Black’ Legends ðŸ”—

This subsection focuses on the emergence of the “Men in Black” legend with Gray Barker’s book They Knew Too Much About Flying Saucers. It discusses the speculation that the men in black are government agents who harass and threaten UFO witnesses.

1960-70s: Growing Distrust in Government ðŸ”—

This section discusses the era of government skepticism that began in the 1960s, following the assassination of John F. Kennedy, the Pentagon Papers, and the Watergate scandal.

Fred Crisman and JFK Conspiracy Theories ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the linking of Fred Crisman to the Kennedy assassination and a supposed UFO cover-up. It mentions Jim Garrison’s prosecution of Clay Shaw and Crisman’s testimony before the New Orleans grand jury. The subsection also notes the United States House Select Committee on Assassinations’ consideration of the possibility that Crisman may have been one of the “three tramps” detained and photographed in the aftermath of the JFK assassination.

This subsection discusses the concept of a “Cosmic Watergate,” which refers to the idea of a government cover-up of UFO information. It mentions the novel and film 2001: A Space Odyssey and J. Allen Hynek’s claim that Bluebook was “a Cosmic Watergate.”

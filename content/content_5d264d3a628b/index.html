UFO Legend Caused by 1947 Balloon Crash: The Roswell Incident ðŸ”—

This article delves into the Roswell incident, a cornerstone of UFO lore, tracing its origins from a 1947 military balloon crash to the elaborate conspiracy theories that persist today.

Newspaper Headline and Incident Overview ðŸ”—

The Roswell Daily Record’s July 8, 1947, issue announced the “capture” of a “flying saucer” by the Roswell Army Air Field (RAAF). The incident occurred between June 4 and July 10, 1947, in Lincoln County, New Mexico. The military balloon debris recovered near Roswell became the basis for conspiracy theories alleging the recovery of an extraterrestrial spacecraft.

1947 Flying Disc Craze ðŸ”—

The Roswell incident occurred during the 1947 flying disc craze, which included events such as the Kenneth Arnold sighting, the Flight 105 sighting, the Rhodes photographs, the Twin Falls hoax, and the Maury Island hoax.

Military Balloon Crash ðŸ”—

In 1947, the United States launched Project Mogul balloons from Alamogordo Army Air Field to detect Soviet atomic tests. On June 4, researchers lost contact with a balloon train near Corona, New Mexico, where rancher W.W. “Mac” Brazel later discovered debris. Initially unaware of the flying disc craze, Brazel consulted his uncle, Hollis Wilson, who suggested the debris might be from a “flying disk.”

Brazel reported his find to Sheriff George Wilcox, who contacted Roswell Army Air Field (RAAF). Major Jesse Marcel and Captain Sheridan Cavitt were assigned to collect the material. RAAF public information officer Walter Haut issued a press release about the recovered “flying disc.” The announcement made international headlines but was retracted within a day, with the army claiming it was a weather balloon.

General Roger Ramey and weather officer Irving Newton identified the material as pieces of a weather balloon at a press conference. The Roswell Daily Record noted the absence of engine or metal parts in the wreckage. Jesse Marcel described the wreckage as “parts of the weather device” composed of “tinfoil and broken wooden beams.” Colonel Marcellus Duffy at Wright Field identified the material as balloon equipment.

UFO Conspiracy Theories (1947â€“1978) ðŸ”—

The 1947 debris retrieval remained relatively obscure for three decades. Belief in UFO cover-ups by the US government became widespread. Hoaxes, legends, and stories of crashed spaceships and alien bodies in New Mexico emerged. The 1947 incident was reinterpreted to fit the public’s increasingly conspiracy-minded outlook.

Aztec Crashed Saucer Hoax ðŸ”—

The 1948 Aztec, New Mexico crashed saucer hoax introduced stories of recovered alien bodies. Con artists convinced Variety columnist Frank Scully to cover their fictitious crash. The hoax narrative included small grey humanoid bodies, metal stronger than any found on Earth, indecipherable writing, and a government coverup.

Hangar 18 ðŸ”—

“Hangar 18” is a non-existent location that many later conspiracy theories allege housed extraterrestrial craft or bodies recovered from Roswell. The idea of alien corpses being stored in an Air Force morgue at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base was mentioned in Scully’s Behind the Flying Saucers, expanded in the 1966 book Incident at Exeter, and became the basis for the 1968 science-fiction novel The Fortec Conspiracy.

In 1974, Robert Spencer Carr alleged that alien bodies recovered from the Aztec crash were stored in “Hangar 18” at Wright-Patterson. The Air Force explained that no “Hangar 18” existed at the base. The 1980 film Hangar 18 dramatized Carr’s claims.

Roswell Conspiracy Theories (1978â€“1994) ðŸ”—

Interest in Roswell was rekindled after ufologist Stanton Friedman interviewed Jesse Marcel in 1978. Marcel stated that the “weather balloon” explanation was a cover story and that he now believed the debris was extraterrestrial. In 1979, Marcel was interviewed by Bob Pratt of the National Enquirer, bringing large-scale attention to the story.

Ufologists interviewed Major Marcel’s son, Jesse A. Marcel Jr. M.D., who said that his father had shown him flying saucer debris, including “a small beam with purple-hued hieroglyphics on it.”

Friedman collaborated with William “Bill” Moore and Charles Berlitz to write the 1980 book The Roswell Incident. It popularized Marcel’s account and added the claimed discovery of alien bodies on the Plains of San Agustin.

The Roswell Incident ðŸ”—

The first Roswell conspiracy book, released in October 1980, was The Roswell Incident by Charles Berlitz and Bill Moore. The book argues that an extraterrestrial craft was flying over the New Mexico desert to observe nuclear weapons activity when a lightning strike killed the alien crew. It alleges that the US government engaged in a cover-up. The book claims that in some photographs, the debris recovered by Marcel had been substituted with the debris from a weather device. The book introduced alien bodies purportedly found by archaeologists on the Plains of San Agustin.

Majestic 12 Hoax ðŸ”—

Majestic 12 was the purported organization behind faked government documents delivered anonymously to multiple ufologists in the 1980s. All individuals who received the fake documents were connected to Bill Moore. After the publication of The Roswell Incident, Richard C. Doty and other individuals presenting themselves as Air Force Intelligence Officers approached Moore. They used the unfulfilled promise of hard evidence of extraterrestrial retrievals to recruit Moore, who kept notes on other ufologists and intentionally spread misinformation within the UFO community. The anonymously delivered documents detailed the creation of a likely fictitious Majestic 12 group formed to handle Roswell debris.

At a 1989 Mutual UFO Network conference, Moore confessed that he had intentionally fed fake evidence of extraterrestrials to UFO researchers, including Paul Bennewitz.

Role of Glenn Dennis ðŸ”—

The initial claims of recovered alien bodies came from the secondhand accounts of “Barney” Barnett and “Pappy” Henderson after their deaths. In 1989, Friedman interviewed former mortician Glenn Dennis, who provided an account of extraterrestrial corpses. Dennis claimed to have received calls from the Air Base with questions about body preservation and inquiries about small or hermetically sealed caskets. In 1991, Dennis co-founded a UFO museum in Roswell along with Max Littell and former RAAF public affairs officer Walter Haut.

Dennis provided false names for the nurse who allegedly witnessed the autopsy.

Competing Accounts and Schism ðŸ”—

A proliferation of competing Roswell accounts led to a schism among ufologists in the early 1990s. The two leading UFO societies disagreed on the scenarios presented by Randleâ€“Schmitt and Friedmanâ€“Berliner.

UFO Crash at Roswell ðŸ”—

In 1991, Kevin Randle and Donald Schmitt published UFO Crash at Roswell. It sold 160,000 copies and served as the basis for the 1994 television film Roswell. Randle and Schmitt added testimony from 100 new witnesses. The book claimed that General Arthur Exon had been aware of debris and bodies, but Exon disputed his depiction.

Crash at Corona ðŸ”—

In 1992, Stanton Friedman released Crash at Corona, co-authored with Don Berliner. The book introduced new “witnesses” and added to the narrative by doubling the number of flying saucers to two, and the number of aliens to eight.

The Truth About the UFO Crash at Roswell ðŸ”—

In 1994, Randle and Schmitt authored another book, The Truth About the UFO Crash at Roswell which claimed a cargo plane delivered alien bodies to Dwight D. Eisenhower. The book abandoned the Barnett crash site on the Plains of San Agustin as lacking evidence. Randle and Schmitt proposed a new crash site 35 miles north of Roswell, based on statements from Jim Ragsdale and Frank Kaufman.

Air Force Response ðŸ”—

The Air Force provided official responses to Roswell conspiracy theories during the mid-1990s under pressure from New Mexico congressman Steven Schiff and the General Accounting Office (GAO). The initial 1994 USAF report admitted that the weather balloon explanation was a cover story for Project Mogul. The following year, The Roswell Report: Fact vs. Fiction in the New Mexico Desert supported this with extensive documentation.

In 1997, the Air Force published a second report, The Roswell Report: Case Closed. It detailed how eyewitness accounts of military personnel loading aliens into “body bags” matched the Air Force’s procedures for retrieving parachute test dummies in insulation bags.

Alien Autopsy ðŸ”—

Pseudo-documentaries, most notably Alien Autopsy: Fact or Fiction, have taken a major role in shaping popular opinion of Roswell. In 1995, British entrepreneur Ray Santilli claimed to have footage of an alien autopsy filmed after the 1947 Roswell crash. In a 2006 documentary, Santilli admitted that the footage was fabricated.

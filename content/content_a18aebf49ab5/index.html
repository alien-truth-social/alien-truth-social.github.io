Unidentified Flying Object (UFO) ðŸ”—

An unidentified flying object (UFO), also known as unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) or unidentified anomalous phenomena, is defined as an object or phenomenon observed in the sky that cannot be immediately identified or explained. While reports of unusual aerial sightings date back to at least the 3rd century BC, the term “UFO” gained prominence after World War II and escalated during the Space Age. Most UFOs are later identified as known objects or atmospheric phenomena, but a small percentage remain unexplained. Government investigations and studies by various organizations have occurred over the years, but without confirmation of claims that UFOs are evidence of extraterrestrial intelligence or other unconventional hypotheses. Skeptics often attribute UFO sightings to natural phenomena, human technology, delusions, or hoaxes. The philosophical implications of UFOs relate to epistemology, dealing with temporarily or permanently unknowable anomalous phenomena. The U.S. government has two entities dedicated to UFO data collection and analysis: NASA’s UAP independent study team and the Department of Defense’s All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office.

Terminology ðŸ”—

During the late 1940s and 1950s, UFOs were commonly referred to as “flying saucers” or “flying discs,” originating from the Kenneth Arnold incident. Captain Edward J. Ruppelt of the USAF coined the acronym “UFO” to replace the misleading term “flying saucer.” The term “unidentified aerial phenomena” (UAP) emerged in the late 1960s and has gained usage in the 21st century due to negative connotations associated with “UFO.” UAP is sometimes expanded to “unidentified anomalous phenomenon.” In popular culture, UFO has become synonymous with alien spacecraft, sometimes referred to as “extra-terrestrial vehicle” (ETV).

Identification ðŸ”—

Most UFOs can be identified as ordinary objects or phenomena after careful investigation. Common identified sources include aircraft, astronomical objects, balloons, hoaxes, light phenomena, other atmospheric objects and phenomena, and psychological effects. The USAF’s 1952â€“1955 study categorized UFOs as balloons, astronomical objects, aircraft, light phenomena, birds, clouds, dust, insufficient information, psychological manifestations, unknown, and other. Allan Hendry, a CUFOS researcher, found that fewer than one percent of cases were hoaxes, with most sightings being misidentifications of prosaic phenomena due to inexperience or misperception. Astronomer Andrew Fraknoi encourages critical thinking when evaluating UFO claims, suggesting that thorough investigation often reveals less to the stories than initially perceived.

History ðŸ”—

Early history before the 20th century ðŸ”—

Throughout history, people have observed unusual sights in the sky, including comets, meteors, planets, planetary conjunctions, and optical phenomena. Halley’s Comet, first recorded by Chinese astronomers, was often seen as a supernatural portent or omen. Some UFO enthusiasts have drawn parallels between religious symbols in medieval paintings and UFO reports, but art historians offer conventional religious interpretations.

Examples of pre-contemporary reports include:

  • Julius Obsequens, a Roman writer, documented wonders and portents in Rome between 249 and 12 BCE, with descriptions resembling meteor showers.
  • Shen Kuo, a Song Chinese scholar-official, described a flying object with opening doors and blinding light in his Dream Pool Essays (1088).
  • A woodcut by Hans Glaser from 1561, known as the “celestial phenomenon over Nuremberg,” has been linked to ancient astronaut claims, though skeptics argue it depicts a sundog.
  • A similar report from 1566 over Basel, along with other leaflets from the 15th and 16th centuries, described “miracles” and “sky spectacles” resembling natural phenomena.
  • In 1878, farmer John Martin reported seeing a large, dark, circular object resembling a saucer, marking one of the first uses of the word “saucer” in association with a UFO.

From November 1896 to April 1897, newspapers reported numerous sightings of “mystery airships,” with some people claiming to have spoken to the pilots. Thomas Edison dismissed these reports as fake.

20th century and after ðŸ”—

During World War II, Allied and Axis pilots reported round, glowing fireballs known as “foo fighters.” Explanations for these sightings included St. Elmo’s fire, the planet Venus, hallucinations, and German secret weapons. In 1946, over 2,000 reports of unidentified aerial objects, referred to as “Russian hail” or “ghost rockets,” were collected, primarily by the Swedish military.

Science fiction depictions of spacecraft similar to flying saucers predated the first widely reported UFO sighting in 1947.

The modern UFO craze began with the Kenneth Arnold sighting on June 24, 1947, when he described seeing “flying saucers” near Mount Rainier. Within weeks, reports of flying saucer sightings became common, including the Roswell incident in 1947. In 1950, influential books by Donald Keyhoe, Frank Scully, and Gerald Heard proposed the extraterrestrial UFO hypothesis.

Media speculation ran rampant, especially during the 1952 UFO scare in Washington, D.C. By 1953, intelligence officials worried that genuine incursions by enemy aircraft could be lost in a flood of UFO reports.

From 1947, the U.S. Air Force began investigating UFO reports through Project Sign, followed by Project Grudge and Project Blue Book. In the late 1950s, public pressure mounted for declassification of UFO records, but the CIA resisted. Official U.S. Air Force interest in UFO reports ended in 1969 after the Condon Report concluded that further investigation was not justified.

From the 1960s to 1990s, UFOs became part of American popular culture’s obsession with the supernatural and paranormal. In 1961, the Barney and Betty Hill alien abduction account gained notoriety. In 1966, 5% of Americans reported seeing something they thought was a “flying saucer.”

In December 2017, media attention increased with the revelation of the secret Advanced Aviation Threat Identification Program, funded from 2007 to 2012. Leaked Pentagon UFO videos led to increased mainstream attention to UFO stories. In July 2021, Harvard astronomer Avi Loeb announced the Galileo Project to seek evidence of extraterrestrial artifacts. Government programs, such as NASA’s UAP independent study team and the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office, were established to investigate UFO claims more fully. In May 2022, the United States House Intelligence Subcommittee held congressional hearings on UAPs, the first public hearing on UFO sightings in the US in over 50 years. Another Congressional hearing took place on July 26, 2023, featuring the whistleblower claims of former U.S. Air Force officer David Grusch.

Polls have shown varying levels of belief in UFOs among Americans, with some indicating that a significant percentage believe UFOs are evidence of alien life.

UFOs have been taken up by religious studies scholars, and popular culture has played a significant role in shaping public perception of extraterrestrials.

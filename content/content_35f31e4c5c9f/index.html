The Roswell Incident is a 1980 book by Charles Berlitz and William Moore that popularized stories of unusual debris recovered in 1947 by personnel from the Roswell Army Air Field. The book is available at the Internet Archive.

Background ðŸ”—

This section provides context for the book by outlining the events of 1947 and the renewed interest in the Roswell incident in the late 1970s.

Events of 1947 ðŸ”—

This section details the initial reports surrounding the Roswell incident, including the Roswell Army Air Field’s press release about recovering a “flying disc” and its subsequent retraction, claiming the object was a weather balloon. It also mentions the broader context of the “flying disc craze” of 1947, sparked by Kenneth Arnold’s alleged sighting.

Roswell revisited ðŸ”—

This section explains how the Roswell story gained renewed attention in 1978 when retired lieutenant colonel Jesse Marcel claimed in an interview with ufologist Stanton Friedman that he believed the debris he retrieved was of extraterrestrial origin.

Authors ðŸ”—

This section provides background information on the authors of the book, Charles Berlitz and William Moore. It notes Berlitz’s previous success with The Bermuda Triangle, a book that popularized the mysteries surrounding that area. It also mentions their collaboration on The Philadelphia Experiment: Project Invisibility, which explored tales of a Navy invisibility experiment and government cover-ups.

Contents ðŸ”—

This section summarizes the book’s central argument: that an extraterrestrial craft crashed in the New Mexico desert while observing nuclear weapons activity, and the US government engaged in a cover-up. It highlights accounts of debris described as “nothing made on this earth” and mentions witnesses like Bill Brazel, Floyd Proctor, and Walt Whitman Jr. The section also notes that the book introduced the idea that the debris shown in photos with Marcel was substituted as part of a cover-up and that the press was not allowed to closely inspect the recovered material. The authors claimed to have interviewed over 90 witnesses, but only a fraction of their testimonies appear in the book. The incarceration of Mac Brazel is also mentioned as a form of witness intimidation. The section also points out that the book elevated Marcel’s narrative over that of Cavitt, whose description of the debris contradicted Marcel’s claims.

Alien bodies ðŸ”—

This section explains that The Roswell Incident was the first book to introduce stories of civil engineer Grady “Barney” Barnett and archaeology students encountering wreckage and “alien bodies” on the Plains of San Agustin, far from the initial debris field near Corona. It also notes that some accounts of the Roswell incident contain information from the Aztec, New Mexico, UFO incident, a hoaxed flying saucer crash.

Reception ðŸ”—

This section summarizes the critical reception of the book, noting that some critics deemed it “a collection of wild hearsay” and questioned the authenticity of the Majestic 12 documents, with some suspicion falling on Moore. It also mentions that the book did not achieve the commercial success its authors had hoped for.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This section mentions that Moore claimed to have engaged in “disinformation” activities against Paul Bennewitz on behalf of the Air Force Office of Special Investigations.

Modern views ðŸ”—

This section references the modern understanding of the Roswell incident. It mentions the 1993 inquiry from US congressman Steven Schiff, which led to a General Accounting Office investigation and an Air Force report concluding that the recovered material was likely debris from Project Mogul, a military surveillance program using high-altitude balloons.

This entry focuses on the 1980 book The Roswell Incident by Charles Berlitz and William Moore, which significantly contributed to popularizing the Roswell incident.

Background ðŸ”—

This section provides context for the Roswell incident, starting with the events of 1947.

Events of 1947 ðŸ”—

This subsection details the initial reports surrounding the Roswell incident. It mentions the flying disc craze of 1947, Kenneth Arnold’s sighting, and the Roswell Army Air Field’s initial press release about recovering a “flying disc,” which was quickly retracted and replaced with the explanation of a weather balloon.

Roswell revisited ðŸ”—

This part highlights the renewed interest in the Roswell story in 1978, sparked by an interview with retired lieutenant colonel Jesse Marcel, who claimed the debris he retrieved was of extraterrestrial origin. Stanton Friedman, a ufologist, conducted the interview.

Authors ðŸ”—

This section provides background information on the authors of the book. Charles Berlitz is noted for authoring The Bermuda Triangle, a bestseller that popularized the mysteries surrounding that area. William L. Moore is identified as a UFO researcher. Their previous collaboration, The Philadelphia Experiment: Project Invisibility, is also mentioned.

Contents ðŸ”—

This section summarizes the book’s central argument: that an extraterrestrial craft crashed in New Mexico while observing nuclear weapons activity, and the US government engaged in a cover-up. It mentions accounts of debris described as “nothing made on this earth” by Jesse Marcel. Other individuals mentioned include Bill Brazel, Floyd Proctor, and Walt Whitman Jr. The book is described as “version 1” of the Roswell myth. It also introduces the idea that the debris shown in photos with Marcel was substituted as part of the cover-up and that the press was not allowed to closely inspect the recovered material. The authors claimed to have interviewed over 90 witnesses, but only a fraction of their testimonies appear in the book. The incarceration of Mac Brazel is also mentioned. The section notes that Marcel’s narrative was elevated above that of Cavitt, whose description of the debris contradicted Marcel’s account.

Alien bodies ðŸ”—

This section discusses the book’s introduction of the “alien bodies” narrative. It mentions the second-hand stories of Grady “Barney” Barnett and archaeology students encountering wreckage and “alien bodies” on the Plains of San Agustin, far from the initial Roswell crash site. It also points out that some accounts of the Roswell incident contain information from the Aztec, New Mexico, UFO incident, a hoax promoted by Frank Scully.

Reception ðŸ”—

This section notes that critics have deemed The Roswell Incident as “a collection of wild hearsay” and that the book “did not make the commercial impact its authors hoped.” It also mentions that fingers were pointed at Moore when critics and skeptics characterized the Majestic 12 documents as fraudulent.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This section mentions that William Moore claimed to have engaged in “disinformation” activities against Paul Bennewitz on behalf of the Air Force Office of Special Investigations at a 1989 MUFON conference.

Modern views ðŸ”—

This section references the 1993 inquiry from US congressman Steven Schiff, which led to a General Accounting Office investigation and an Air Force report. The 1994 Air Force report concluded that the material recovered was likely debris from Project Mogul, a military surveillance program using high-altitude balloons.

This entry details the 1980 book The Roswell Incident by Charles Berlitz and William Moore, which significantly contributed to popularizing the Roswell incident.

Background ðŸ”—

This section provides context for the Roswell incident, focusing on the events of 1947 and the renewed interest in the story in the late 1970s.

Events of 1947 ðŸ”—

This subsection outlines the initial reports of the Roswell incident during the “flying disc craze” of 1947, Kenneth Arnold’s sighting, the Roswell Army Air Field’s initial press release about recovering a crashed disc, and the subsequent retraction stating it was a weather balloon.

Roswell revisited ðŸ”—

This subsection explains how the Roswell story gained traction again in 1978 when retired lieutenant colonel Jesse Marcel claimed in an interview with ufologist Stanton Friedman that the debris he recovered was extraterrestrial.

Authors ðŸ”—

This section provides background information on the authors of the book, Charles Berlitz and William Moore. It mentions Berlitz’s previous success with The Bermuda Triangle and their collaboration on The Philadelphia Experiment: Project Invisibility.

Contents ðŸ”—

This section summarizes the book’s central argument: that an extraterrestrial craft crashed in New Mexico while observing nuclear weapons activity, and the US government engaged in a cover-up. It highlights accounts of debris described as “nothing made on this earth” and mentions witnesses like Bill Brazel, Floyd Proctor, and Walt Whitman Jr. The section also notes the book’s introduction of the idea that the debris shown in photos with Marcel was substituted as part of the cover-up. It also mentions the claim that the military sought to discredit the growing interest in flying saucers. The section also notes that the authors claimed to have interviewed over 90 witnesses, but only a fraction of those accounts appear in the book. The section also notes that the book elevated Marcel’s narrative above that of Cavitt, whose description of the debris contradicted Marcel.

Alien bodies ðŸ”—

This section details the book’s introduction of the story about Barney Barnett and archaeology students encountering wreckage and “alien bodies” on the Plains of San Agustin. It also mentions the conflation of the Roswell incident with the Aztec, New Mexico, UFO incident, a hoaxed flying saucer crash promoted by Frank Scully.

Reception ðŸ”—

This section discusses the reception of the book, noting that critics deemed it “a collection of wild hearsay” and that it “did not make the commercial impact its authors hoped.” It also mentions accusations against Moore regarding the Majestic 12 documents.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This section mentions Moore’s admission at a 1989 MUFON conference that he had engaged in “disinformation” activities against Paul Bennewitz on behalf of the Air Force Office of Special Investigations.

Modern views ðŸ”—

This section references the 1993 inquiry by the General Accounting Office in response to US congressman Steven Schiff, which led to a 1994 Air Force report concluding that the recovered material was likely debris from Project Mogul, a military surveillance program using high-altitude balloons.

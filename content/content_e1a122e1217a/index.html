This article discusses the history, description, and cultural impact of “flying saucers,” a term popularized in the late 1940s to describe disc-shaped unidentified flying objects (UFOs). It distinguishes flying saucers from the broader category of UFOs/UAPs and explores their evolution in popular culture.

History ðŸ”—

This section traces the origins and development of the flying saucer phenomenon.

Precursors ðŸ”—

This subsection explores earlier reports of unusual aerial phenomena that predate the term “flying saucer.” These include:

  • Mysterious lights in the sky interpreted as spiritual phenomena in antiquity.
  • 19th-century newspaper reports of massive airships.
  • The 1878 Denison Daily News article describing an object resembling a saucer.
  • “Foo fighters” reported by Allied pilots during World War II.
  • Science fiction stories featuring disc-shaped aircraft, such as Jean de La Hire’s 1908 novel La Roue fulgurante and “The Shaver Mystery” stories in Amazing Stories magazine.
  • Fantasy artwork in pulp magazines depicting flying discs.

Origins ðŸ”—

This subsection details the event that is credited with popularizing the term “flying saucer”:

  • The Kenneth Arnold UFO sighting on June 24, 1947, in Washington State.
  • Arnold’s description of the objects’ motion as “like a saucer skipping across the water.”
  • The media’s coining of the terms “flying saucer” and “flying disc.”
  • The subsequent wave of reported sightings across the United States, including the Flight 105 UFO sighting and the Roswell incident.
  • Early speculation about the objects’ origins, including secret military technology and Soviet aircraft.

Development ðŸ”—

This subsection covers the evolution of the flying saucer concept and its association with extraterrestrial life:

  • The increasing association of flying saucers with extraterrestrial life in the 1950s.
  • Kenneth Arnold’s speculation about an extraterrestrial origin for the objects.
  • The emergence of unusual theories, such as Meade Layne’s speculation about alternate dimensions.
  • The influence of pulp science fiction on beliefs about flying saucers.
  • The rise of the “contactee” movement, with individuals like George Van Tassel and George Adamski claiming to have ridden aboard saucers and received messages from aliens.
  • The development of ufology as a parallel social movement, with figures like Frank Scully, Donald Keyhoe, and Leonard H. Stringfield publishing books and forming UFO investigation groups.
  • The emergence of UFO photography as a subgenre of documentary photography.
  • The eventual decline in popularity of the term “flying saucer” and its replacement by “UFO” and “UAP.”

Description ðŸ”—

This section describes the characteristics and identification of flying saucers.

Identification ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the identification of reported flying saucers with known phenomena:

  • Experts have identified the majority of UFO reports as misidentifications or hoaxes.
  • Common explanations for saucer sightings include the planet Venus, weather phenomena, balloons, and airborne trash.
  • The US Navy’s Skyhook spy balloon project, which led to numerous misidentified sightings.
  • Psychological studies of why people believe in flying saucers, including Georges Heuyer’s view of the phenomenon as a shared delusion and Michel Monnerie’s psychosocial UFO hypothesis.

Reported sightings ðŸ”—

This subsection summarizes the characteristics of reported flying saucer sightings:

  • Eyewitness descriptions differ in reported appearance, movement, and purpose.
  • Astronomer Donald Howard Menzel’s overview of flying saucers, noting the variety of shapes, sizes, colors, and movements reported.
  • The increasing standardization of alien descriptions over time, with Grey aliens becoming the most reported type of pilot.
  • The influence of popular social and cultural expectations on the details of claimed observations.

Fictional portrayals ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the portrayal of flying saucers in popular media:

  • Early portrayals emphasized high-speed maneuvers, while later media shifted to slowly hovering discs.
  • Examples of films and television series that feature flying saucers, such as The Flying Saucer, The Day the Earth Stood Still, and The Invaders.

Popular culture ðŸ”—

This section explores the cultural impact of flying saucers.

Literature ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the appearance of flying saucers in literature:

  • Precursors to modern flying saucers appeared in science fiction literature, including The Shaver Mystery.
  • The emergence of a genre that treated fantastical stories as either true or plausibly true.
  • The use of advertisements to leverage cultural interest in flying saucers.
  • The appearance of aliens and flying discs in 1950s science fiction comics.

Film and television ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the portrayal of flying saucers in film and television:

  • Many early portrayals of flying saucers linked them to the Cold War.
  • Examples of films that feature flying saucers, such as The Thing from Another World, The Day the Earth Stood Still, and Earth vs. the Flying Saucers.
  • The adaptation of the flying saucer phenomenon by other countries, such as Japan and India.
  • The use of flying saucers in other genres, such as science fiction and comedy.

Architecture ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the use of flying saucer motifs in architecture:

  • The flying saucer as a symbol of 1950s culture.
  • The use of flying saucer motifs in Googie architecture and Atomic Age dÃ©cor.
  • Notable flying saucer structures, such as Seattle’s Space Needle and Los Angeles International Airport’s Theme Building.
  • The influence of Googie architecture on the futuristic structures in the cartoon The Jetsons.
  • The use of the flying saucer as an architectural motif by Frank Lloyd Wright.
  • Examples of novelty architecture shaped like flying saucers, such as the Kielce Bus Station in Poland and the Spodek arena in Katowice, Poland.

Broader pop culture ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the broader cultural impact of flying saucers:

  • Flying saucers were a ubiquitous part of pop culture from 1947 into the mid-1970s.
  • The use of flying disc motifs in toys and other novelties, such as the frisbee and flying saucer candy.
  • The release of novelty songs about flying discs and alien invaders.
  • The appearance of flying saucers in video games, typically as antagonists.

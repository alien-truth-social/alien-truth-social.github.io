Cultural Impact of Extraterrestrial Contact ðŸ”—

This article explores the potential changes to human society, science, technology, religion, politics, and ecosystems that could result from contact with an extraterrestrial civilization. It emphasizes that the nature and magnitude of these changes would depend on factors such as the extraterrestrial civilization’s technological advancement, intentions (benevolent or malevolent), and the level of mutual understanding between the two species. The article also draws parallels to historical encounters between vastly different human cultures on Earth, such as the Columbian Exchange, and encounters between native and invasive species.

Background ðŸ”—

This section sets the stage by introducing the concept of the search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI) and its relationship to analyzing the implications of contact. It highlights that SETI focuses on locating intelligent life, while this article focuses on the potential consequences of such contact. It acknowledges that tragic consequences are largely speculative due to the absence of verified public contact to date.

Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence ðŸ”—

This section details the efforts to detect extraterrestrial civilizations using radio telescopes. It mentions the challenge of identifying artificial signals against the background of natural radio waves. Key locations and projects mentioned include the Allen Telescope Array in Hat Creek, California, the Five hundred meter Aperture Spherical Telescope (FAST) in China, the now-demolished Arecibo Observatory in Puerto Rico, Project Cyclops commissioned by NASA, and Messaging to Extra-Terrestrial Intelligence (METI). The section also discusses the discovery of numerous extrasolar planets, including potentially habitable ones like Kepler-22b, and the renewed government interest in funding SETI programs, such as the European Space Agency’s COROT and NASA’s Kepler space observatory. Projects like A Message From Earth (AMFE) and SETILive are also mentioned, along with alternative search methods like SEVENDIP at the University of California, Berkeley. The section concludes by acknowledging that while several signals, such as the Wow! signal, have been detected, none have been confirmed as being of intelligent origin.

Impact Assessment ðŸ”—

This section discusses methods for assessing the potential impact of extraterrestrial contact. It introduces the Rio scale, which provides a quantitative picture of the results of extraterrestrial contact based on factors such as the method of discovery, the information content of messages, and the nature of the extraterrestrial beings. The section also mentions a study by astronomer Steven J. Dick at the United States Naval Observatory, which analyzes events of similar significance in the history of science to predict the cultural impact of extraterrestrial contact. The study argues that the impact would be most strongly influenced by the information content of the message received and distinguishes between short-term and long-term impacts. The section also discusses the importance of physical distance between civilizations, with closer contact likely to be more disruptive. It also mentions a poll of United States and Chinese university students in 2000, which provides factor analysis of responses to questions about the participants’ belief that extraterrestrial life exists in the Universe, that such life may be intelligent, and that humans will eventually make contact with it. The section concludes by mentioning a survey by psychologists Douglas Vakoch and Yuh-shiow Lee to assess people’s reactions to receiving a message from extraterrestrials, including their judgments about likelihood that extraterrestrials would be malevolent.

Post-Detection Protocols ðŸ”—

This section outlines the protocols that have been developed to guide scientists and governments in the event of extraterrestrial contact. It emphasizes that these protocols are not legally binding and are likely to be ignored if contact occurs. The section mentions the “Declaration of Principles for Activities Following the Detection of Extraterrestrial Intelligence,” created by the SETI Permanent Committee of the International Academy of Astronautics (IAA) and endorsed by various international scientific unions. The Declaration of Principles contains broad provisions, including verification of the signal’s intelligent origin, communication with other signatories of the Declaration, informing national authorities, informing the astronomical community, making news of the discovery public, publishing all data, minimizing terrestrial use of the electromagnetic frequency bands, and not responding to the signal without international agreement. The section also mentions a separate “Proposed Agreement on the Sending of Communications to Extraterrestrial Intelligence,” which proposes an international commission to decide whether to send a message and determine its contents. The section concludes by noting that Paul Davies, a member of the SETI Post-Detection Taskgroup, believes that post-detection protocols are unlikely to be followed by astronomers.

Contact Scenarios and Considerations ðŸ”—

This section explores various models of interaction between extraterrestrial and human civilizations, ranging from beneficial collaborations to hostile subjugation. It highlights the importance of the extraterrestrial civilization’s level of aggressiveness, ethics, and biological compatibility with humans. The section also considers whether contact is via signals or physical probes, and the impact of mutual comprehension.

Friendly Civilizations ðŸ”—

This section discusses potential interactions with benevolent extraterrestrial civilizations. It mentions Albert Harrison, a professor emeritus of psychology at the University of California, Davis, who suggests that advanced civilizations might share knowledge such as a physical theory of everything, zero-point energy, or faster-than-light travel. The section also mentions Seth D. Baum, of the Global Catastrophic Risk Institute, who suggests that cooperative civilizations are more likely to aid humanity. In contrast, Paolo Musso, a member of the SETI Permanent Study Group of the International Academy of Astronautics (IAA) and the Pontifical Academy of Sciences, takes the view that extraterrestrial civilizations possess, like humans, a morality driven not entirely by altruism but for individual benefit as well, thus leaving open the possibility that at least some extraterrestrial civilizations are hostile. The section also discusses Allen Tough’s suggestions for how a friendly civilization might help humanity, including intervention to avert catastrophe, advice and action with consent, and forcible corrective action. The section concludes by noting that even with friendly contact, humans could suffer from a loss of identity and confidence, and that a friendly civilization may avoid contact altogether to allow natural development, known as the zoo hypothesis.

Hostile Civilizations ðŸ”—

This section explores the potential dangers of contact with aggressive extraterrestrial civilizations. It notes that while science fiction often depicts humans successfully repelling alien invasions, scientists generally believe that a technologically advanced civilization could easily destroy human civilization. The section mentions operations that are enormous on a human scale, such as destroying all major population centers on a planet, bombarding a planet with deadly neutron radiation, or even traveling to another planetary system in order to lay waste to it, may be important tools for a hostile civilization. The section also discusses the views of Carl Sagan, who believed that a civilization capable of interstellar travel would have transcended war, and Seth Shostak, who argues that limited resources would cultivate aggression. Ragbir Bhathal claims that an extremely advanced extraterrestrial civilization may have the motivation to colonize humanity in a similar manner to the European colonization of much of the rest of the world. The section also mentions David Brin, who states that while an extraterrestrial civilization may have an imperative to act for no benefit to itself, it would be naÃ¯ve to suggest that such a trait would be prevalent throughout the galaxy. The section concludes by noting that humans may be an unsuitable food source for extraterrestrials due to biochemical differences, and that transmitting intentional signals may not increase the risk of invasion, as any civilization capable of interstellar travel could already detect Earth’s accidental radio leakage.

Equally Advanced and More Advanced Civilizations ðŸ”—

This section examines the implications of contact with civilizations of similar or greater technological advancement. It mentions Robert Freitas’ speculation that the technological advancement and energy usage of a civilization, measured by its rating on the Kardashev scale, may play an important role in the result of extraterrestrial contact. The section also discusses Ben Finney’s comparison of the situation to the cargo cults of New Guinea, where indigenous people misunderstood European technology. The section concludes by noting that John Billingham suggests that a civilization which is far more technologically advanced than humanity is also likely to be culturally and ethically advanced, and would therefore be unlikely to conduct astroengineering projects that would harm human civilization.

Interstellar Groups of Civilizations ðŸ”—

This section explores the possibility of “galactic clubs,” groupings of civilizations from across the galaxy. It mentions Albert Harrison’s surmise that such clubs might exist, beginning as loose confederations and eventually developing into powerful unions. The section also discusses Sebastian von Hoerner’s suggestion that entry into a galactic club may be a way for humanity to handle the culture shock arising from contact with an advanced extraterrestrial civilization. The section concludes by noting that Michael A. G. Michaud suggests that an interstellar grouping of civilizations might take the form of an empire, which need not necessarily be a force for evil, but may provide for peace and security throughout its jurisdiction.

Extraterrestrial Artifacts ðŸ”—

This section discusses the possibility of extraterrestrial civilizations communicating through artifacts or probes rather than radio. It notes that probes can hold sustained dialogues and perform surveillance, and that Allen Tough speculates that the enormous amount of energy required to achieve such speeds under currently proposed mechanisms means that robotic probes traveling at conventional speeds will still have an advantage for various applications. The section also mentions Robert Freitas’ reasons why interstellar probes may be a preferred method of communication, including the ability to survey non-intelligent populations, store information for long timescales, and emit strong, unambiguous signals. The section concludes by noting that given the age of the Milky Way galaxy, an ancient extraterrestrial civilization may have existed and sent probes to the Solar System millions or even billions of years before the evolution of Homo sapiens.

Roswell Incident ðŸ”—

The Roswell incident refers to the events surrounding the crash of a military balloon near Roswell, New Mexico, in 1947. This event later became the basis for widespread conspiracy theories alleging the recovery of an extraterrestrial spacecraft by the United States military. The article explores the initial event, the subsequent development of UFO conspiracy theories, and the official explanations provided by the United States Air Force.

UFO Legend Caused by 1947 Balloon Crash ðŸ”—

This section introduces the Roswell incident, highlighting the initial newspaper headline that reported the “capture” of a “flying saucer” by the Roswell Army Air Field (RAAF). It sets the stage for the timeline of events, which occurred between June 4 and July 10, 1947, in Lincoln County, New Mexico. The section also mentions the “1947 flying disc craze” and lists related events such as the Kenneth Arnold sighting, the Flight 105 sighting, and various hoaxes.

Military Balloon Debris Recovered in 1947 ðŸ”—

This section details the initial event, explaining that military balloon debris was recovered near Roswell, New Mexico, in 1947. It clarifies that this debris later became the basis for conspiracy theories about a crashed extraterrestrial spacecraft. The balloon was part of the top-secret Project Mogul, operated from Alamogordo Army Air Field, designed to detect Soviet nuclear tests. The United States Army initially announced the possession of a “flying disc” but retracted the statement within a day, attributing the debris to a conventional weather balloon to obscure the true purpose.

1947 Military Balloon Crash ðŸ”—

This section provides a detailed account of the events leading up to the Roswell incident. It explains that by 1947, the United States had launched thousands of top-secret Project Mogul balloons to listen for Soviet atomic tests. On June 4, researchers at Alamogordo Army Air Field in New Mexico launched a long train of these balloons, which they lost contact with near Corona, New Mexico, where W.W. “Mac” Brazel’s ranch was located. Brazel discovered tinfoil, rubber, tape, and thin wooden beams scattered across his ranch later that month.

Brazel, initially unaware of the “flying disc craze,” visited Corona on July 5, where his uncle suggested the debris could be from a “flying disk.” The next day, Brazel informed Sheriff George Wilcox of his discovery, who then contacted Roswell Army Air Field (RAAF). Major Jesse Marcel and Captain Sheridan Cavitt were assigned to gather the material. On July 8, RAAF public information officer Walter Haut issued a press release stating that the military had recovered a “flying disc” near Roswell.

The section also describes the press conference where General Roger Ramey, Colonel Thomas DuBose, and weather officer Irving Newton identified the material as pieces of a weather balloon.

UFO Conspiracy Theories (1947â€“1978) ðŸ”—

This section explains that the 1947 debris retrieval remained relatively obscure for three decades. Reporting ceased after the government provided a mundane explanation, and broader reporting on flying saucers declined rapidly after the Twin Falls saucer hoax. Despite this, belief in UFO cover-ups by the US government became widespread. Hoaxes, legends, and stories of crashed spaceships and alien bodies in New Mexico emerged that later formed elements of the Roswell myth.

Aztec Crashed Saucer Hoax ðŸ”—

This section introduces the Aztec, New Mexico crashed saucer hoax in 1948, which introduced stories of recovered alien bodies that later became associated with Roswell. The hoax narrative included small grey humanoid bodies, metal stronger than any found on Earth, indecipherable writing, and a government coverup to prevent public panic.

Hangar 18 ðŸ”—

This section discusses “Hangar 18,” a non-existent location that many later conspiracy theories allege housed extraterrestrial craft or bodies recovered from Roswell. The idea of alien corpses from a crashed ship being stored in an Air Force morgue at the Wright-Patterson Air Force Base was mentioned in Scully’s Behind the Flying Saucers, expanded in the 1966 book Incident at Exeter, and became the basis for a 1968 science-fiction novel The Fortec Conspiracy.

Roswell Conspiracy Theories (1978â€“1994) ðŸ”—

This section details the rekindling of interest in Roswell after ufologist Stanton Friedman interviewed Jesse Marcel in 1978. Marcel stated that the “weather balloon” explanation from the press conference was a cover story and that he now believed the debris was extraterrestrial. The tabloid National Enquirer brought large-scale attention to the Marcel story the following February.

The Roswell Incident ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the first Roswell conspiracy book, The Roswell Incident by Charles Berlitz and Bill Moore, released in October 1980. The book argues that an extraterrestrial craft was flying over the New Mexico desert to observe nuclear weapons activity when a lightning strike killed the alien crew. It alleges that, after recovering the crashed alien technology, the US government engaged in a cover-up to prevent mass panic.

Majestic 12 Hoax ðŸ”—

This section discusses Majestic 12, the purported organization behind faked government documents delivered anonymously to multiple ufologists in the 1980s. The anonymously delivered documents detailed the creation of a likely fictitious Majestic 12 group formed to handle Roswell debris.

Role of Glenn Dennis ðŸ”—

This section introduces Glenn Dennis, a former mortician who provided an account of extraterrestrial corpses endorsed by prominent Roswell ufologists. Dennis claimed to have received calls from the Air Base with questions about body preservation and inquiries about small or hermetically sealed caskets.

UFO Crash at Roswell ðŸ”—

This section focuses on Kevin Randle and Donald Schmitt’s book, UFO Crash at Roswell, published in 1991. The book claimed that General Arthur Exon had been aware of debris and bodies, and it included Glenn Dennis’s claims of an alien autopsy and Grady Barnett’s “alien body” accounts.

Crash at Corona ðŸ”—

This section discusses Stanton Friedman’s book, Crash at Corona, co-authored with Don Berliner, released in 1992. The book introduced new “witnesses” and added to the narrative by doubling the number of flying saucers to two, and the number of aliens to eight.

The Truth About the UFO Crash at Roswell ðŸ”—

This section focuses on Randle and Schmitt’s 1994 book, The Truth About the UFO Crash at Roswell, which claimed a cargo plane delivered alien bodies to Dwight D. Eisenhower. The book abandoned the Barnett crash site on the Plains of San Agustin and proposed a new crash site 35 miles north of Roswell.

Air Force Response ðŸ”—

This section details the Air Force’s official responses to Roswell conspiracy theories during the mid-1990s. The initial 1994 USAF report admitted that the weather balloon explanation was a cover story for Project Mogul, a military surveillance program. The following year, The Roswell Report: Fact vs. Fiction in the New Mexico Desert supported this with extensive documentation that narrowed the cause of the debris to a specific Mogul balloon train launched on June 4, 1947, and lost near the Roswell debris field.

Alien Autopsy ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the pseudo-documentary Alien Autopsy: Fact or Fiction, which claimed to have footage of an alien autopsy filmed after the 1947 Roswell crash.

The Day After Roswell ðŸ”—

This section discusses Philip J. Corso’s book, The Day After Roswell, released in 1997. Corso alleged that he was shown a purportedly nonhuman body suspended in liquid inside a glass coffin and that he helped oversee a project to reverse engineer recovered crash debris.

This section provides explanations for the Roswell incident, stating that secrecy around the 1947 debris recovery was due to Cold War military programs rather than aliens. It notes that UFO believers maintain that a spacecraft crashed near Roswell, and “Roswell” remains synonymous with UFOs.

This section identifies the crashed object from the 1947 incident as a Project Mogul device. Mogul was a military surveillance program employing high-altitude balloons to monitor nuclear tests.

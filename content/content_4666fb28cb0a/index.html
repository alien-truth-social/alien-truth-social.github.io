Time-traveler UFO hypothesis ðŸ”—

The time-traveler hypothesis, also known as chrononaut UFO, future humans, extratempestrial model, and Terminator theory, proposes that unidentified flying objects (UFOs) are humans traveling from the future using advanced technology. This concept has gained recent attention from figures like retired NASA aerospace engineer Larry Lemke, Wisconsin congressman Mike Gallagher, and filmmaker Steven Spielberg. Mainstream scholars consider this hypothesis extremely implausible, and it’s generally viewed as unorthodox even among fringe conspiracy theorists.

This section explores the origins of the ideas that led to the time-traveler UFO hypothesis.

Origins of time travel speculation ðŸ”—

This subsection traces the concept of time travel from the future to the past back to its literary origins. Pierre Boitard’s 1861 book Paris avant les hommes is credited with introducing the idea, featuring a man sent back to prehistoric Earth. Camille Flammarion’s 1887 novel Lumen also explored the theme of a soul traveling through different historical periods. The section also notes that while time travel is considered physically impossible by most, theoretical research continues to explore its potential feasibility, with scientists like J. Richard Gott and Ronald Mallet speculating on the possibility of future human time travel.

Speculation of future human evolution ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the history of speculation about the future evolution of mankind. It highlights H.G. Wells’ The Time Machine as an early example of both time travel and speculative evolution. Other authors mentioned include Edgar Rice Burroughs, Olaf Stapledon, Kurt Vonnegut, Stephen Baxter, and Cevdet Mehmet KÃ¶semen. The section also identifies climate change, the technological singularity, space travel, and genetic enhancement as potential factors that could alter human evolution, leading to transhuman and posthuman scenarios.

UFOs and initial explanations ðŸ”—

This subsection provides a brief overview of the history of UFO sightings. It mentions reports of unknown objects in the skies dating back to antiquity, as well as the mystery airship sightings of the 1890s and the Foo fighters and ghost rockets during World War II. The modern UFO era is said to have begun in the summer of 1947, following Kenneth Arnold’s sighting. Initial explanations for these sightings ranged from novel US or Soviet technology to misidentification of mundane objects and mass hysteria. Fringe theories included extraterrestrial or interdimensional origins.

Formation and spread ðŸ”—

This section details the development and popularization of the time-traveler UFO hypothesis.

Implausibility of humanoid aliens ðŸ”—

This subsection addresses the common depiction of aliens as humanoids in contactee and alien abduction folklore. It cites George Gaylord Simpson’s 1964 article The Nonprevalence of Humanoids, which argues that it’s unlikely for extraterrestrial intelligence to exist and even more unlikely for it to be humanoid. The section also mentions Stephen Jay Gould’s thought experiment about rewinding evolution, suggesting that humans would likely not evolve again. Biologist Jack Cohen’s view on the implausibility of Grey aliens evolving a physiology similar to humans is also included.

Modern popularization ðŸ”—

This subsection explores the modern resurgence of the time-traveler hypothesis. It mentions Jacques VallÃ©e’s 1975 book The Invisible College as an early example. Other figures and works mentioned include David S. Anderson, Jenny Randles, James Herbert Brennan, and Philip J. Corso and William J. Birnes’ The Day After Roswell, which reported Hermann Oberth’s belief that the Roswell craft could have been a time machine. The History channel series Ancient Aliens is also mentioned for its episodes exploring the concept. Michael P. Masters’ book Identified Flying Objects and The Extratempestrial Model are highlighted for their in-depth exploration of the hypothesis. Tim Peake and Steven Spielberg are mentioned for their recent public comments on the theory.

In popular culture ðŸ”—

This subsection lists examples of the time-traveler hypothesis in popular culture. It includes the TV series It’s About Time, Gregory Benford’s novel Timescape, David Drake’s novel Birds of Prey, Michael Crichton’s novel Sphere, the TV film Official Denial, the comic book series Blake and Mortimer, the band Yes, Jordan Peele’s movie Nope, and the movie Interstellar.

Terminology and connection to other theories ðŸ”—

This subsection clarifies the terminology used to describe the hypothesis, noting that while Michael P. Masters uses the term “extratempestrial,” the overall idea is often referred to as “the time-traveler hypothesis.” It also discusses the overlap between the time-traveler hypothesis and the interdimensional hypothesis, with figures like John Ventre and Harold E. Puthoff using the terms interchangeably. The section also notes that the time-traveler hypothesis provides an alternative explanation for the absence of future time travelers, similar to how the extraterrestrial hypothesis addresses Fermi’s paradox.

Criticism ðŸ”—

This section presents criticisms of the time-traveler hypothesis. It notes that the hypothesis is considered pseudoscientific. Neil deGrasse Tyson is quoted as calling the hypothesis illogical, and Robert Sheaffer criticizes Michael P. Masters’ work for relying on the belief that time travel is possible. David J. Darling argues that the extraterrestrial and time-travel hypotheses are equally unlikely and unnecessary. Ian Ridpath suggests that Jim Penniston’s claims about time-traveling UFOs may have been influenced by the film Official Denial. Isaac Arthur is mentioned for debunking the concept as presented in Ancient Aliens. Fabio Costa is quoted as saying that time travel requires two doors, one in the future and one in the past, raising the question of where the time machine is.

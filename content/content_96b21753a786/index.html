Ancient Astronauts ðŸ”—

Ancient astronauts, also known as ancient aliens, is a pseudoscientific belief that intelligent extraterrestrial beings visited Earth in ancient times and influenced the development of human civilization. Proponents suggest that these aliens affected modern cultures, technologies, religions, and human biology. A common idea is that deities from religions are extraterrestrial in origin, and advanced technologies were seen as divine by early humans. Academics and archaeologists dismiss these claims as pseudoarchaeological or unscientific, lacking credible peer-reviewed studies. Some argue that these theories have racist undertones, diminishing the accomplishments of indigenous cultures.

Overview ðŸ”—

Various terms are used to describe claims about ancient astronauts, including ancient aliens, ancient ufonauts, ancient space pilots, paleocontact, astronaut- or alien gods, and paleo- or biblical-SETI. Believers often claim that humans are descendants or creations of extraterrestrial intelligence who landed on Earth in the past. They also suggest that human knowledge, religion, and culture originated from these ancient extraterrestrial visitors, who acted as a “mother culture.” Proponents argue that aliens built or aided in building structures like the Egyptian pyramids and the Moai stone heads of Easter Island. Evidence is said to come from documentary gaps in historical and archaeological records, as well as artifacts believed to be anachronistic and beyond the technical capabilities of ancient people.

The idea is considered pseudoarcheology and is featured in fringe media like the History Channel series Ancient Aliens. Such shows mix fact with fiction to promote theories of an alternate past, often aligning with white supremacist, nativist, imperialist, settler-colonial, and Christian Identity beliefs. Proponents portray themselves as an oppressed minority of academics, claiming a conspiracy by ‘big archaeology’ to disenfranchise them. They focus on monumental archaeological structures, implying that non-white indigenous people could not have built them without extraterrestrial intervention. However, some indigenous voices argue that the idea of extraterrestrials supports their own origin stories. A common feature is the portrayal of aliens as light-skinned or Aryan, as claimed by Erich von DÃ¤niken.

Paleocontact narratives first appeared in early science fiction of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Harold T. Wilkins proposed the idea in earnest in 1954. It gained popularity in the 1960s due to the Space Race and the success of Erich von DÃ¤niken’s works. Critics emerged in the 1970s, discrediting Von DÃ¤niken’s claims. Carl Sagan and Soviet astrophysicist Iosif Shklovsky co-authored Intelligent Life in the Universe in 1966. Sagan later expressed disapproval of writers like von DÃ¤niken who built on their ideas without caution, presenting them as valid evidence of extraterrestrial contact. Erich von DÃ¤niken ðŸ”—

Erich von DÃ¤niken became a leading proponent of the ancient astronaut hypothesis in the late 1960s and early 1970s, gaining a large audience through his book Chariots of the Gods? According to von DÃ¤niken, certain artifacts required more sophisticated technology than was available to the ancient cultures that constructed them. He maintained that these artifacts were constructed either directly by extraterrestrial visitors or by humans who learned the necessary knowledge from them. These include Stonehenge, Pumapunku, the Moai of Easter Island, the Great Pyramid of Giza, and the ancient Baghdad electric batteries.

Von DÃ¤niken wrote that ancient art and iconography throughout the world illustrate air and space vehicles, non-human but intelligent creatures, ancient astronauts, and artifacts of anachronistically advanced technology. He also stated that geographically separated historical cultures share artistic themes, implying a common origin. One example is his interpretation of the sarcophagus lid from the tomb of Pacal the Great in Palenque, which he saw as representing a seated astronaut. He interpreted the origins of many religions as reactions to encounters with an alien race, viewing the aliens’ technology as supernatural and the aliens themselves as gods.

Critics argue that von DÃ¤niken misrepresented data, that many of his claims were unfounded, and that none of his core claims have been validated.

Zecharia Sitchin ðŸ”—

Zecharia Sitchin’s series The Earth Chronicles, beginning with The 12th Planet, revolves around Sitchin’s interpretation of ancient Sumerian and Middle Eastern texts, megalithic sites, and artifacts. He hypothesized that the gods of old Mesopotamia were astronauts from the planet “Nibiru,” which the Sumerians believed to be a remote “12th planet” associated with the god Marduk. According to Sitchin, Nibiru continues to orbit the Sun on a 3,600-year elongated orbit.

Sitchin argued that Sumerian texts tell the story that 50 Anunnaki from Nibiru came to Earth approximately 400,000 years ago to mine raw materials, especially gold. They eventually created Homo sapiens sapiens: the “Adapa” (model man) or Adam of later mythology. Sitchin contended the Anunnaki were active in human affairs until their culture was destroyed by global catastrophes caused by the abrupt end of the last ice age some 12,000 years ago.

Sitchin’s work has not received mainstream scholarly support and has been criticized by professionals. Semitic languages scholar Michael S. Heiser says that many of Sitchin’s translations of Sumerian and Mesopotamian words are not consistent with Mesopotamian cuneiform bilingual dictionaries.

Robert Temple ðŸ”—

Robert K. G. Temple’s 1976 book, The Sirius Mystery, argues that the Dogon people of northwestern Mali preserved an account of extraterrestrial visitation from around 5,000 years ago. He quotes various lines of evidence, including advanced astronomical knowledge inherited by the tribe, descriptions, and comparative belief systems with ancient civilizations such as ancient Egypt and Sumer. His work draws heavily on the studies of cultural anthropologists Marcel Griaule and Germaine Dieterlen.

His conclusions have been criticized by scientists, who point out discrepancies within Temple’s account, and suggested that the Dogon may have received some of their astronomical information recently, probably from European sources, and may have misrepresented Dogon ethnography.

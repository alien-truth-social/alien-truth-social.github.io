Extraterrestrial Life ðŸ”—

Extraterrestrial life, also known as alien life, refers to life that originates from a world other than Earth. While no such life has been conclusively detected, the possibility ranges from simple prokaryotes to intelligent beings and civilizations. The science dedicated to studying extraterrestrial life is astrobiology.

Context ðŸ”—

This section provides a framework for understanding the topic of extraterrestrial life within the broader context of life in the universe. It outlines related topics such as planetary habitability within and outside the Solar System, including specific locations like Venus, Mars, Europa, Enceladus, and Titan. It also touches on the habitability of various star systems, including binary star systems, those with natural satellites, neutron stars, red dwarfs, K-type main-sequence stars, yellow dwarfs, and F-type main-sequence stars.

Likelihood of Existence ðŸ”—

This section explores the debate surrounding the prevalence of life in the cosmos. It presents the argument for ubiquitous extraterrestrial life, emphasizing the size and age of the universe, the commonality of life’s building blocks, and the universality of physical laws. Scientists like Carl Sagan and Stephen Hawking are mentioned as proponents of this view, which aligns with the Copernican and mediocrity principles.

The opposing view, the Rare Earth hypothesis, suggests that life, especially complex life, may be rare due to a unique combination of factors specific to Earth. The Drake equation, devised by Frank Drake, is introduced as a probabilistic tool for estimating the number of communicative extraterrestrial civilizations in the Milky Way. However, its controversial nature is highlighted due to the speculative nature of its variables. The Fermi paradox, the apparent contradiction between the high probability of extraterrestrial civilizations and the lack of evidence for them, is also mentioned. Dennis W. Sciama, Zhi-Wei Wang, and Samuel L. Braunstein are mentioned in relation to the Fermi Paradox.

Biochemical Basis ðŸ”—

This section delves into the potential biochemical makeup of extraterrestrial life. It acknowledges the possibility of diverse life forms, from microorganisms to intelligent beings, and uses Earth’s life as a reference point. The section discusses the requirements for life, including non-equilibrium thermodynamics and a source of energy, such as stars, volcanoes, or hydrothermal vents.

The importance of a liquid solvent, particularly water, for biochemical reactions is emphasized, while also considering alternatives like ammonia or liquid hydrocarbons. The section explores potential alternative chemical elements for life, focusing on carbon and its possible replacements like silicon. The section references Norman Horowitz and his work at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory. It also touches on the likelihood of cell membranes and the process of evolution, referencing Simon Conway Morris and his ideas on convergent evolution.

Harsh Environmental Conditions on Earth Harboring Life ðŸ”—

This section highlights the discovery of life thriving in extreme environments on Earth, such as hydrothermal vents and acidic hot springs. These environments, once considered unlikely to harbor life, provide parallels to the harsh conditions found on other planets and support the possibility of extraterrestrial life. The section emphasizes the importance of chemical reactions, such as chemosynthesis, in these environments and their potential relevance to life on other planets with reducing atmospheres.

Planetary Habitability in the Solar System ðŸ”—

This section assesses the potential for life on various bodies within our Solar System. Besides Earth, Mars, Europa, and Enceladus are identified as the most promising candidates. Venus is discussed as a terrestrial planet that took a different evolutionary path. Mars is considered a possibility due to past water abundance. The gas and ice giants are deemed unlikely to host life, but their moons are of interest. Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto are mentioned as having subsurface oceans, with Europa being the most promising due to its ocean’s contact with the rocky surface. Enceladus, a moon of Saturn, is highlighted for its eruption columns that release water into space. Titan, another moon of Saturn, is noted for having liquid bodies on its surface composed of hydrocarbons. Mary Voytek is mentioned as an astrobiologist.

Scientific Search ðŸ”—

This section outlines the scientific approach to searching for extraterrestrial life, primarily through astrobiology. It emphasizes the interdisciplinary nature of the field, encompassing astronomy, biology, chemistry, geology, oceanography, and atmospheric sciences. The search is conducted both directly, by looking for biosignatures, and indirectly, by searching for habitable exoplanets.

Search for Basic Life ðŸ”—

This section details the search for biosignatures within the Solar System and beyond. It mentions the controversial report of nanobacteria-like structures in the ALH84001 meteorite, which sparked the development of astrobiology. The Viking Mars landers’ gas emission experiments are discussed, along with NASA scientists’ claims of methane signatures on Mars. The Mars Science Laboratory and the Curiosity rover are mentioned as tools for assessing past and present habitability on Mars. A catalog of microorganisms being developed at Cornell University is mentioned. The section also discusses the discovery of organic molecules in meteorites found in Antarctica and complex organic compounds in cosmic dust. Sun Kwok of the University of Hong Kong is mentioned.

Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligences ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI) through the detection of technosignatures. It outlines three main types of technosignatures: interstellar communications, atmospheric effects, and planetary-sized structures. Organizations like the SETI Institute are mentioned, along with their search for radio waves and laser pulses. The challenges of distinguishing natural signals from artificial ones are discussed, as well as the potential use of artificial intelligence in this process.

Extrasolar Planets ðŸ”—

This section discusses the search for exoplanets, particularly terrestrial planets within habitable zones. It notes the discovery of thousands of exoplanets and the role of the Kepler space telescope in this effort. The section highlights the estimate that at least one in five Sun-like stars has an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone. Proxima Centauri b is mentioned as the nearest known exoplanet. The section also mentions the least and most massive exoplanets known, as well as the study of planetary habitability.

History and Cultural Impact ðŸ”—

This section explores the historical and cultural context of the extraterrestrial life debate.

Cosmic Pluralism ðŸ”—

This section traces the historical roots of the idea of extraterrestrial life back to ancient Greece. It contrasts the views of the atomists, like Epicurus, who believed in multiple worlds, with those of the Aristotelians, who believed Earth was unique. The section mentions Anaximander, Apollo, and the Great Library of Alexandria. It also touches on the influence of religious views on the debate.

Early Modern Period ðŸ”—

This section discusses the impact of the Copernican Revolution and the invention of the telescope on the concept of extraterrestrial life. It mentions Nicolaus Copernicus, Tycho Brahe, Johannes Kepler, Galileo Galilei, and Isaac Newton. It highlights Giordano Bruno as an early proponent of extraterrestrial life and his persecution for his beliefs.

19th Century ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the 19th-century speculation about life on Mars, fueled by observations of apparent Martian canals. It mentions Percival Lowell and his books on Mars, as well as William Wallace Campbell’s spectroscopic analysis of Mars’s atmosphere. The section also discusses the disproving of spontaneous generation by Louis Pasteur and the rise of the panspermia theory.

Recent History ðŸ”—

This section discusses the emergence of astrobiology as a scientific discipline. It mentions NASA, ESA, and INAF as organizations involved in astrobiology research. The section also touches on the rise of pseudoscience, such as ufology and the ancient astronauts hypothesis. Lewis White Beck is mentioned for his work on the philosophical thought on extraterrestrial life. It also discusses the views of scientists like Frank Drake and Jacques Monod. Stephen Hawking and Jared Diamond are mentioned for their warnings about contacting alien life forms. Yuri Milner and the Breakthrough Initiatives are also mentioned.

Government Responses ðŸ”—

This section outlines the government policies and international agreements related to extraterrestrial life. It mentions the 1967 Outer Space Treaty and the 1979 Moon Agreement, as well as COSPAR guidelines for planetary protection. The United Nations Office for Outer Space Affairs’ discussions on strategies for interacting with extraterrestrial life are mentioned. NASA’s Planetary Protection Office is also highlighted. The space programs of China and Russia are mentioned. Isaac Ben-Israel and Haim Eshed are mentioned in relation to the Israel Space Agency.

In Fiction ðŸ”—

This section explores the portrayal of extraterrestrial life in fiction. It discusses the impact of Charles Darwin’s theory of evolution on the depiction of aliens and the influence of real-life events, such as the Barney and Betty Hill incident, on alien archetypes.

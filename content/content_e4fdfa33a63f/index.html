Alien (film) ðŸ”—

Alien is a 1979 science fiction horror film directed by Ridley Scott and written by Dan O’Bannon. The film follows the crew of the commercial spaceship Nostromo as they encounter a deadly extraterrestrial creature. The film stars Tom Skerritt, Sigourney Weaver, Veronica Cartwright, Harry Dean Stanton, John Hurt, Ian Holm, and Yaphet Kotto.

Plot ðŸ”—

The commercial spaceship Nostromo is returning to Earth when its computer detects a transmission from a nearby planetoid. The crew investigates a derelict alien spaceship and discovers a chamber containing hundreds of eggs. One of the crew members, Kane, is attacked by a creature that attaches itself to his face. The creature eventually detaches itself, and Kane appears to be fine. However, during a meal, a small alien creature bursts from his chest, killing him. The crew then tries to hunt down and kill the alien, which grows into a deadly creature. The crew members are killed off one by one, until only Ripley remains. She initiates the ship’s self-destruct sequence and escapes in a shuttle, but the alien stows away. Ripley manages to blast the alien into space before putting herself into stasis for the return to Earth.

Cast ðŸ”—

  • Tom Skerritt as Dallas, captain of the Nostromo.
  • Sigourney Weaver as Ripley, the warrant officer.
  • Veronica Cartwright as Lambert, the navigator.
  • Harry Dean Stanton as Brett, the engineering technician.
  • John Hurt as Kane, the executive officer.
  • Ian Holm as Ash, the science officer who is revealed to be an android.
  • Yaphet Kotto as Parker, the chief engineer.
  • Bolaji Badejo as the alien.
  • Helen Horton as the voice of Mother, the Nostromo’s computer.

Production ðŸ”—

Writing ðŸ”—

Dan O’Bannon and Ronald Shusett collaborated on the screenplay, with O’Bannon initially writing 29 pages titled Memory. The story was influenced by various science fiction and horror works, including The Thing from Another World, Forbidden Planet, and Planet of the Vampires. The script was pitched as “Jaws in space”. Hill and Giler made revisions to the script, adding the android character Ash.

Development ðŸ”—

20th Century-Fox greenlit Alien after the success of Star Wars. Ridley Scott was chosen as the director. Scott created detailed storyboards, which led to the film’s budget being doubled.

Casting ðŸ”—

Casting calls were held in New York City and London. Scott wanted strong actors to focus on the film’s visual style. The crew of the Nostromo was intended to resemble working astronauts, described as “truckers in space”. Scott wrote backstories for each character.

Filming ðŸ”—

Filming took place at Pinewood Studios and Shepperton Studios near London, and model filming was done at Bray Studios. The production schedule was short due to the film’s low budget. Sets were constructed for the alien planetoid, the Nostromo, and the derelict spacecraft. The space suits were thick and bulky, causing discomfort for the actors.

Post-production ðŸ”—

Editing and post-production took roughly 20 weeks. The film was edited to have a slow pace to build suspense. One scene that was cut involved Ripley encountering Dallas and Brett, who had been partially cocooned by the alien.

Music ðŸ”—

Jerry Goldsmith composed the musical score. Scott initially wanted Isao Tomita, but Fox wanted a more familiar composer. Goldsmith’s score was nominated for a Golden Globe Award and a Grammy Award, and it won a BAFTA Award.

Design ðŸ”—

Creature effects ðŸ”—

H. R. Giger designed the alien and its environment. The “facehugger” was the first creature Giger designed. The “chestburster” design was inspired by Francis Bacon’s painting Three Studies for Figures at the Base of a Crucifixion.

The alien ðŸ”—

Giger made conceptual paintings of the adult alien before settling on the final version. Carlo Rambaldi manufactured the head. Bolaji Badejo portrayed the alien in most scenes. Scott chose not to show the full alien for most of the film to create a sense of terror.

Sets ðŸ”—

The sets of the Nostromo’s three decks were created in one piece. Ron Cobb created industrial-style symbols and color-coded signs. The company that owns the Nostromo is referred to as “Weylan-Yutani”. Art director Roger Christian used scrap metal and parts to create set pieces and props.

Spaceships and planets ðŸ”—

Ron Cobb and Chris Foss worked on designs for the spaceship and space suits. Cobb created sketches of the ship’s interiors and exteriors. The spaceships and planets were shot using models and miniatures.

Title sequence ðŸ”—

The title sequence was developed by R/Greenberg Associates.

Release ðŸ”—

Alien was rated “R” in the United States and “X” in the United Kingdom. It opened in a limited release in American theaters on May 25, 1979, and received a wide release on June 22.

Box office ðŸ”—

The film was a commercial success, grossing $78.9 million in the United States and Â£7,886,000 in the United Kingdom during its first run. Its total worldwide gross has been estimated at between $104.9 million and $203.6 million.

Critical reception ðŸ”—

Critical reaction to Alien was initially mixed. However, in subsequent years, it was critically reassessed and is now considered one of the greatest science fiction and horror films of all time.

Accolades ðŸ”—

Alien won the Academy Award for Best Visual Effects and Saturn Awards for Best Science Fiction Film, Best Direction for Ridley Scott, and Best Supporting Actress for Veronica Cartwright.

Post-release ðŸ”—

Home video ðŸ”—

Alien has been released in many home video formats over the years, including VHS, LaserDisc, and DVD.

Director’s Cut ðŸ”—

In 2003, 20th Century Fox released a “Director’s Cut” of Alien, which included restored footage and recut scenes.

Cinematic analysis ðŸ”—

Critics have analyzed Alien’s sexual overtones and its use of abjection. The film has been interpreted as a metaphor for male fear of penetration and as a representation of the “monstrous-feminine”.

Audience research ðŸ”—

Findings from an international audience research project were published in 2016 as Alien Audiences: Remembering and Evaluating a Classic Movie.

Re-release ðŸ”—

For its 45th anniversary, Alien was re-released in theaters on April 26, 2024.

Legacy ðŸ”—

Critical reassessment ðŸ”—

Despite initial mixed reviews, Alien has received critical acclaim over the years and is seen as one of the most influential science-fiction films.

Cultural influences ðŸ”—

Alien had both an immediate and long-term impact on the science fiction and horror genres. The “chestburster” effect was parodied in Mel Brooks’s comedy Spaceballs. Nintendo’s Metroid video game series was significantly influenced by Alien.

Merchandise ðŸ”—

Alan Dean Foster wrote a novelization of the film. Heavy Metal magazine published a graphic novel adaptation. Model kits and action figures of the alien were released.

School play adaptation ðŸ”—

In 2019, students at North Bergen High School adapted the film into a play, which gained social media recognition and praise from Ridley Scott and Sigourney Weaver.

Video game adaptation ðŸ”—

Two downloadable content packs for the video game Alien: Isolation depict alternate versions of key events from the film, with the original cast members reprising their roles.

Sequels and franchise ðŸ”—

The success of Alien led to three direct sequels: Aliens (1986), Alien 3 (1992), and Alien Resurrection (1997). The story of Ripley’s encounters with the aliens became the thematic and narrative core of the series. The film series resulted in a media franchise with novels, comic books, video games, and merchandise. A prequel series was started with Prometheus (2012) and continued with Alien: Covenant (2017).

Extraterrestrial Life ðŸ”—

Extraterrestrial life, also known as alien life, refers to life that originates from a world other than Earth. While no conclusive scientific detection has occurred, the possibilities range from simple prokaryotes to intelligent beings and advanced civilizations. The field dedicated to its study is astrobiology.

Context ðŸ”—

This section provides background information on the conditions necessary for life in the universe. It starts with the Big Bang and the subsequent cooling of the universe, highlighting the initial lack of elements necessary for life. The formation of carbon and oxygen through stellar fusion is mentioned, as well as the emergence of planetary systems and organic compounds. Abiogenesis and panspermia are presented as potential origins of life. The section also discusses the vastness of the universe, the limitations of current space exploration technology, and the concept of the circumstellar habitable zone (Goldilocks zone). It concludes by noting the potential for past extraterrestrial life and the adaptive capabilities of life on Earth, while emphasizing that habitability does not guarantee the existence of life. Avi Loeb is mentioned for his speculation on the “Habitable Epoch of the Early Universe”.

Likelihood of Existence ðŸ”—

This section explores the debate on whether extraterrestrial life is common or rare. It presents the argument for ubiquitous life based on the size and age of the universe, the abundance of life’s chemical elements, and the universality of physical laws, citing Carl Sagan and Stephen Hawking. The Copernican and mediocrity principles are also mentioned. The Rare Earth hypothesis, which posits that a unique combination of factors makes Earth habitable, is presented as a counterargument. The Drake equation, devised by Frank Drake, is introduced as a probabilistic argument to estimate the number of communicative extraterrestrial civilizations in the Milky Way galaxy. The section concludes by discussing the Fermi paradox, which highlights the contradiction between the high probability estimates of extraterrestrial civilizations and the lack of evidence for their existence. Dennis W. Sciama, Zhi-Wei Wang, and Samuel L. Braunstein are mentioned in relation to the Fermi paradox.

Biochemical Basis ðŸ”—

This section discusses the potential biochemical characteristics of extraterrestrial life. It considers a range of lifeforms from microorganisms to intelligent beings. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics, stars, volcanoes, plate tectonics, and hydrothermal vents are presented as potential energy sources. Water is highlighted as a crucial solvent, though alternatives like ammonia and hydrocarbons are mentioned. The section explores the chemical elements that could compose extraterrestrial life, focusing on carbon and its potential replacements like silicon. The advantages and disadvantages of silicon-based life are discussed. Norman Horowitz is mentioned for his views on carbon-based life. The section also considers the potential for radically different biochemistries, the role of natural selection, and the likelihood of cell membranes. The evolution from simple cells to multicellular organisms is discussed, with Simon Conway Morris’s views on convergent evolution mentioned. Scientists from the University of Oxford are mentioned for their study suggesting aliens may be similar to humans.

Harsh Environmental Conditions on Earth Harboring Life ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the origins of life on Earth and how it may be related to the possibility of extraterrestrial life. It highlights that the conditions on other planets seem too extreme to harbor life, but some of the earliest and most basic forms of life on Earth originated in extreme environments. Fossil evidence and historical theories have marked environments like hydrothermal vents or acidic hot springs as some of the first places that life could have originated on Earth. The aspects of these harsh environments that make them ideal for the origin of life on Earth, as well as the possibility of creation of life on other planets, is the chemical reactions forming spontaneously.

Planetary Habitability in the Solar System ðŸ”—

This section examines the potential for life on other bodies within our solar system. Mars, Europa, and Enceladus are identified as the most likely candidates. Venus is discussed as a terrestrial planet that may have once been similar to Earth. The possibility of microbial life in Venus’s high-altitude clouds is mentioned. Mars is described as a cold, airless desert, but with evidence of past abundant water. The potential for ancient fossilized remains and surviving microbes underground is noted. The gas giants and ice giants are deemed unlikely to contain life, but the possibility of life on moons orbiting these planets is explored. Europa’s subsurface ocean is highlighted, as well as Ganymede and Callisto. Enceladus’s eruption columns are mentioned as a potential source of study. Titan is presented as the only celestial body besides Earth with liquid bodies on the surface, with speculations about lifeforms with different biochemistry.

Scientific Search ðŸ”—

This section outlines the scientific methods used to search for extraterrestrial life. Astrobiology is defined as the science that studies life in the universe. The search is carried out both directly and indirectly, with the identification of exoplanets being a key component. The section mentions the number of identified exoplanets and systems as of September 2017 and February 2021.

Search for Basic Life ðŸ”—

This section details the search for biosignatures within the solar system and beyond. It mentions the study of planetary surfaces, meteorites, and telescope data. The controversial report of nanobacteria-like structures in the ALH84001 meteorite is discussed. The Viking Mars landers’ gas emission experiments are mentioned, along with the methane signatures found in Mars’s atmosphere. NASA scientists Carol Stoker and Larry Lemke are mentioned in this context. The Mars Science Laboratory and the Curiosity rover are highlighted as tools for assessing past and present habitability on Mars. A group of scientists at Cornell University are mentioned for starting a catalog of microorganisms, with the way each one reacts to sunlight. NASA’s study of meteorites found on Antarctica is mentioned, with the discovery of adenine, guanine, hypoxanthine, and xanthine. Scientists reported that cosmic dust contains complex organic compounds. Astronomers at Copenhagen University reported the detection of glycolaldehyde in a distant star system. Astronomers reported the first time discovery, in the plumes of Enceladus, moon of the planet Saturn, of hydrogen cyanide.

Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligences ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the search for extraterrestrial intelligence through technosignatures. Interstellar communications, atmospheric effects, and planetary-sized structures like Dyson spheres are mentioned as potential indicators. Organizations like the SETI Institute are searching for radio waves and laser pulses. The use of artificial intelligence in this search is mentioned. The section also discusses the potential for detecting nitrogen dioxide, chlorofluorocarbons, and light pollution in exoplanet atmospheres. The Kardashev scale is introduced as a measure of a civilization’s energy consumption.

Extrasolar Planets ðŸ”—

This section discusses the search for exoplanets that may be conducive to life, focusing on terrestrial planets within habitable zones. The number of exoplanets discovered is mentioned, along with the Kepler space telescope’s contributions. The section notes that there is at least one planet on average per star, and that about 1 in 5 Sun-like stars have an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone. Proxima Centauri b is mentioned as the nearest known exoplanet. The least massive and most massive exoplanets known are mentioned. The section concludes by noting the importance of planetary habitability in determining the suitability of a planet for hosting life.

History and Cultural Impact ðŸ”—

This section explores the historical and cultural context of the search for extraterrestrial life.

Cosmic Pluralism ðŸ”—

This section traces the historical development of the idea of cosmic pluralism, the belief in numerous inhabited worlds beyond Earth. It begins with the Ancient Greeks, contrasting the atomist view of Epicurus with the Aristotelian view. Medieval Muslim writers Fakhr al-Din al-Razi and Muhammad al-Baqir are mentioned for supporting cosmic pluralism based on the Qur’an. Chaucer’s poem The House of Fame is mentioned. The section also discusses the association of atomism with atheism and the varying views within the Church. Anaxagoras is mentioned for the term ‘panspermia’.

Early Modern Period ðŸ”—

This section discusses the impact of the Copernican Revolution and the invention of the telescope on the concept of extraterrestrial life. Nicolaus Copernicus, Tycho Brahe, Johannes Kepler, and Galileo Galilei are mentioned for their contributions to the heliocentric model. Galileo’s trial for heresy is noted. Giordano Bruno is highlighted as an early proponent of extraterrestrial life, who was tried and executed for his beliefs. Isaac Newton’s theory of gravity is mentioned as further strengthening the heliocentric model.

19th Century ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the 19th-century speculation about life on Mars, fueled by telescopic observations of apparent Martian canals. Percival Lowell is mentioned for his books proposing that the canals were the work of a civilization. William Wallace Campbell is mentioned for his spectroscopic analysis of Mars’s atmosphere. Louis Pasteur’s disproving of spontaneous generation is noted. JÃ¶ns Jacob Berzelius, Kelvin, Hermann von Helmholtz, and Svante Arrhenius are mentioned for their panspermia theory. The development of science fiction is mentioned.

Recent History ðŸ”—

This section discusses the emergence of astrobiology as a distinct science. NASA, ESA, and INAF are mentioned as organizations studying astrobiology. The limitations of studying only Earth-based life are noted. The rise of pseudoscience, such as ufology and ancient astronauts, is mentioned. Lewis White Beck is mentioned for tracing the evolution of philosophical thought. The section concludes by noting the advances in planetary habitability knowledge, astronomy, and spacecraft technology. Frank Drake and Jacques Monod are mentioned. Peter Ward and Donald Brownlee are mentioned for their book Rare Earth: Why Complex Life is Uncommon in the Universe.

Extraterrestrial Life ðŸ”—

Extraterrestrial life, also known as alien life, refers to life that originates from a world other than Earth. While no such life has been conclusively detected, it could range from simple prokaryotes to intelligent beings and civilizations. The study of extraterrestrial life falls under the domain of astrobiology.

Context ðŸ”—

This section provides a framework for understanding the topic of life in the universe. It outlines the conditions necessary for life to arise, starting from the Big Bang and the formation of elements like carbon and oxygen through stellar fusion. It touches on the concepts of planetary systems, habitable zones, and the vast distances involved in studying extraterrestrial life. The section also mentions the role of abiogenesis and panspermia in the origin and spread of life.

Likelihood of Existence ðŸ”—

This section delves into the debate about whether life in the cosmos is common or rare. It presents the argument for ubiquitous extraterrestrial life, based on the size and age of the universe, the abundance of life’s building blocks, and the universality of physical laws. Key figures like Carl Sagan and Stephen Hawking are mentioned as proponents of this view. The opposing Rare Earth hypothesis, which posits that a unique combination of factors makes Earth exceptionally suited for life, is also discussed. The Drake equation, developed by Frank Drake, is introduced as a probabilistic tool for estimating the number of communicative extraterrestrial civilizations in the Milky Way, while acknowledging its controversial and speculative nature. The Fermi paradox, the apparent contradiction between the high probability of extraterrestrial civilizations and the lack of evidence for them, is also mentioned.

Biochemical Basis ðŸ”—

This section explores the potential biochemical foundations of extraterrestrial life. It acknowledges that life could range from microorganisms to intelligent beings. It discusses the requirements for life, including non-equilibrium thermodynamics, a solvent (primarily water), and essential elements like carbon. Alternative solvents like ammonia and hydrocarbons are also considered. The section examines the possibility of different biochemistries, including the potential for silicon-based life, while noting the advantages of carbon. It also touches on the role of natural selection, replication, and cell membranes in the evolution of life.

Harsh Environmental Conditions on Earth Harboring Life ðŸ”—

This section highlights the discovery of life thriving in extreme environments on Earth, such as hydrothermal vents and acidic hot springs. These environments, once considered uninhabitable, provide parallels to the harsh conditions found on other planets and support the possibility of extraterrestrial life existing in similar extreme conditions. The section emphasizes the importance of chemical reactions, such as chemosynthesis, in sustaining life in these environments, particularly in the absence of oxygen.

Planetary Habitability in the Solar System ðŸ”—

This section assesses the potential for life on various bodies within our solar system. Besides Earth, Mars, Europa, and Enceladus are identified as the most promising candidates. Venus is discussed as a terrestrial planet that may have once been similar to Earth. Mars is considered a potential site for past or present microbial life. The subsurface oceans of Europa, Ganymede, Callisto, and Enceladus are explored as possible habitats. Titan, with its hydrocarbon lakes and subsurface ocean, is also mentioned as a unique environment that could potentially support life with different biochemistry.

Scientific Search ðŸ”—

This section outlines the scientific methods used to search for extraterrestrial life. Astrobiology, the science dedicated to this search, combines astronomy, biology, chemistry, geology, and other disciplines. The search encompasses both direct and indirect methods, including the analysis of telescope data, specimen data, and the search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI).

Search for Basic Life ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the search for biosignatures, indicators of life, within the solar system and beyond. It mentions past claims of evidence for microbial life on Mars, including structures resembling nanobacteria in the ALH84001 meteorite and gas emissions detected by the Viking landers. The Mars Science Laboratory and the Curiosity rover are highlighted as ongoing efforts to assess the habitability of Mars. The section also mentions the creation of a catalog of microorganisms and their reactions to sunlight to aid in the search for similar organisms on exoplanets. The discovery of organic molecules, including nucleobases and sugars, in meteorites and cosmic dust is presented as evidence that the building blocks of life may be widespread in the universe. The detection of hydrogen cyanide in the plumes of Enceladus is also mentioned as a possible chemical essential for life.

Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligences ðŸ”—

This section discusses the search for extraterrestrial intelligence (SETI) through the detection of technosignatures, indicators of technology. It mentions the SETI Institute’s search for radio waves and laser pulses. It also explores the possibility of detecting atmospheric effects, such as air pollution, and planetary-sized structures, such as Dyson spheres. The Green Bank Telescope is mentioned as one of the radio telescopes used by the Breakthrough Listen project.

Extrasolar Planets ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the search for exoplanets, particularly terrestrial planets within the habitable zones of their stars. It notes the discovery of thousands of exoplanets and the role of the Kepler space telescope in this effort. The section mentions the estimate that at least one in five Sun-like stars has an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone. Proxima Centauri b is identified as the nearest known exoplanet. The section also discusses the analysis of exoplanet atmospheres for signs of oxygen and other biosignatures.

History and Cultural Impact ðŸ”—

This section explores the historical and cultural context of the search for extraterrestrial life.

Cosmic Pluralism ðŸ”—

This section traces the philosophical belief in numerous worlds beyond Earth, known as cosmic pluralism, back to ancient Greece. It contrasts the atomist view of Epicurus, who believed that other worlds with their own animals and plants should exist, with the Aristotelian view that Earth was the only planet in the universe. The section also mentions the support for cosmic pluralism in ancient Jainism and by medieval Muslim writers.

Early Modern Period ðŸ”—

This section discusses the impact of the Copernican Revolution and the heliocentric model on the concept of extraterrestrial life. It mentions Nicolaus Copernicus, Tycho Brahe, Johannes Kepler, and Galileo Galilei as key figures in this shift. Giordano Bruno is highlighted as an early proponent of the idea of an infinite universe with countless planetary systems. The section also notes the resistance to these new ideas from the Catholic Church.

19th Century ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the increased speculation about life on Mars in the late 19th century, fueled by telescopic observations of apparent Martian canals. Percival Lowell is mentioned as a prominent proponent of the idea that these canals were the work of a civilization. The section also notes the spectroscopic analysis of Mars’s atmosphere, which revealed the absence of water and oxygen. The disproving of spontaneous generation by Louis Pasteur is mentioned, as well as the rise of the science fiction genre and its influence on public perception of extraterrestrial life.

Recent History ðŸ”—

This section discusses the development of astrobiology as a scientific discipline. It mentions NASA, ESA, and INAF as organizations involved in astrobiology research. The section also notes the emergence of pseudosciences, such as ufology and the ancient astronauts hypothesis, in response to public interest in extraterrestrial life. It also mentions the Rare Earth hypothesis, which argues that complex life is uncommon in the universe. The section concludes by noting the ongoing scientific efforts to search for extraterrestrial life and the technological advances that may make it possible to detect it.

Government Responses ðŸ”—

This section outlines the government policies and actions related to extraterrestrial life. It mentions the Outer Space Treaty and the Moon Agreement as defining rules of planetary protection. COSPAR is also mentioned as providing guidelines for planetary protection. The section notes that the UN lacks response mechanisms for the case of an extraterrestrial contact. NASA’s Office of Safety and Mission Assurance (OSMA) is highlighted for its mission to prevent backward contamination of Earth by extraterrestrial life. The space programs of China, Russia, France, and Israel are also mentioned as having objectives related to the search for extraterrestrial life.

In Fiction ðŸ”—

This section explores the portrayal of extraterrestrial life in fiction. It notes that early depictions of aliens were often similar to humans, but that the theory of evolution led to the creation of more diverse and bizarre aliens. The section mentions the influence of real-life events, such as the Barney and Betty Hill incident, on the depiction of aliens in fiction, particularly the grey alien archetype.

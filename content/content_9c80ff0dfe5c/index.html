Extraterrestrial Life ðŸ”—

Extraterrestrial life, also known as alien life, refers to life that originates from another world rather than Earth. While no extraterrestrial life has been conclusively detected, the possibility ranges from simple prokaryotes to intelligent beings and civilizations. The science dedicated to studying extraterrestrial life is astrobiology.

Context ðŸ”—

This section provides a framework for understanding the topic of life in the universe. It lists various celestial bodies within and outside the Solar System that are considered potentially habitable, including Venus, Mars, Europa, Enceladus, and Titan. It also touches on the conditions necessary for life, such as the presence of liquid water and a suitable atmosphere. The section explains that the universe was initially too hot for life after the Big Bang and that the necessary elements for life, like carbon and oxygen, appeared later through stellar fusion. It also introduces the concepts of abiogenesis and panspermia as potential origins and dispersal mechanisms for life.

Likelihood of Existence ðŸ”—

This section explores the debate over whether life in the cosmos is common or rare. It presents the argument for ubiquitous extraterrestrial life, emphasizing the size and age of the universe, the ubiquity of life’s chemical elements, and the universality of physical laws. Scientists like Carl Sagan and Stephen Hawking are mentioned as proponents of this view, which is supported by the Copernican and mediocrity principles. The Rare Earth hypothesis, which posits that life on Earth is possible due to a series of unlikely factors, is also discussed. Frank Drake and his Drake equation, a probabilistic argument used to estimate the number of communicative extraterrestrial civilizations in the Milky Way, are introduced. The section concludes by mentioning the Fermi paradox, which highlights the contradiction between the high probability estimates of extraterrestrial civilizations and the lack of evidence for their existence. Dennis W. Sciama, Zhi-Wei Wang, and Samuel L. Braunstein are mentioned in relation to the Fermi paradox.

Biochemical Basis ðŸ”—

This section delves into the potential biochemical characteristics of extraterrestrial life. It discusses the basic requirements for life, such as non-equilibrium thermodynamics and a source of energy, like stars, volcanoes, or hydrothermal vents. The importance of water as a solvent is highlighted, along with the possibility of alternative solvents like ammonia or liquid hydrocarbons. The section also explores alternative chemical elements that could compose life, focusing on carbon and silicon. Norman Horowitz is mentioned for his views on carbon-based life. The section further discusses the potential for radically different biochemistries, the role of natural selection, and the likelihood of cell membranes and evolution. Simon Conway Morris is mentioned for his views on convergent evolution.

Harsh Environmental Conditions on Earth Harboring Life ðŸ”—

This section addresses the common perception that harsh conditions on other planets make them unlikely to harbor life. It presents evidence that some of the earliest and most basic forms of life on Earth originated in extreme environments, such as hydrothermal vents and acidic hot springs. These environments are characterized by intense UV radiation, extreme temperatures, and a lack of water. The section highlights the chemosynthetic processes occurring around hydrothermal vents, which allow organisms to utilize energy through reduced chemical compounds that fix carbon. It suggests that similar processes could occur on other planets with reducing atmospheres and result in the origin of extraterrestrial life.

Planetary Habitability in the Solar System ðŸ”—

This section examines the potential for life on various bodies within the Solar System. Besides Earth, Mars, Europa, and Enceladus are identified as the most likely places to find life. Venus is discussed as a terrestrial planet that may have once been similar to Earth. Mars is described as a cold desert, but with evidence of past water abundance. The gas giants are deemed unlikely to contain life, but their moons, such as Europa and Enceladus, are considered promising due to the presence of subsurface oceans. Titan, with its liquid hydrocarbon bodies, is also mentioned as a potential location for life with different biochemistry. Mary Voytek is mentioned for her views on the likelihood of finding large ecosystems.

Scientific Search ðŸ”—

This section outlines the scientific methods used to search for extraterrestrial life. Astrobiology, the science that studies life in the universe, is introduced. The section mentions the direct and indirect searches for extraterrestrial life, including the identification of thousands of exoplanets. It also mentions studies considering the possible detection of lifeforms on Venus and Mars.

Search for Basic Life ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the search for biosignatures, or evidence of life, within the Solar System and beyond. It mentions the controversial report of nanobacteria-like structures in the ALH84001 meteorite from Mars. The Viking Mars landers’ gas emission experiments are also discussed. NASA scientists’ claims of methane signatures on Mars are mentioned, along with the Mars Science Laboratory and the Curiosity rover. A catalog of microorganisms being developed at Cornell University is mentioned as a tool for searching for life on exoplanets. The discovery of organic molecules in meteorites and cosmic dust is also highlighted. The detection of glycolaldehyde, a sugar molecule, in a distant star system is mentioned as a sign that complex organic molecules may form in stellar systems prior to planet formation. The discovery of hydrogen cyanide in the plumes of Enceladus is also mentioned.

Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligences ðŸ”—

This section shifts the focus to the search for extraterrestrial intelligence. It discusses the concept of technosignatures, or effects on a planet caused by technology. The three main types of technosignatures considered are interstellar communications, effects on the atmosphere, and planetary-sized structures. Organizations like the SETI Institute are mentioned for their search for potential forms of communication. The challenges of distinguishing between natural and artificial signals are highlighted. The section also discusses potential atmospheric technosignatures, such as nitrogen dioxide, chlorofluorocarbons, and light pollution. The Kardashev scale and the possibility of detecting Dyson spheres are also mentioned.

Extrasolar Planets ðŸ”—

This section discusses the search for exoplanets that may be conducive to life. It mentions the discovery of thousands of exoplanets, ranging in size from Earth-like to gas giants. The Kepler space telescope is mentioned for its detection of thousands of candidate planets. The section highlights the estimate that at least one in five Sun-like stars have an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone. The nearest known exoplanet, Proxima Centauri b, is mentioned. The least massive and most massive exoplanets known are also noted. The section concludes by discussing the importance of planetary habitability and the analysis of exoplanet atmospheres for signs of oxygen.

This section explores the historical and cultural context of the search for extraterrestrial life.

Cosmic Pluralism ðŸ”—

This section traces the historical development of the idea of cosmic pluralism, the belief in numerous worlds beyond Earth. It begins with the ancient Greeks, highlighting the contrasting views of the atomists, like Epicurus, and the Aristotelians. Medieval Muslim writers like Fakhr al-Din al-Razi and Muhammad al-Baqir are mentioned for their support of cosmic pluralism. Chaucer’s poem The House of Fame is also mentioned. The section discusses the association of atomism with atheism and the varying views within the Church. The first known mention of the term ‘panspermia’ by Anaxagoras is also mentioned.

Early Modern Period ðŸ”—

This section discusses the Copernican Revolution and its impact on the notion of extraterrestrial life. Nicolaus Copernicus, Tycho Brahe, Johannes Kepler, and Galileo Galilei are mentioned for their contributions to the heliocentric model. Giordano Bruno is highlighted as an early proponent of extraterrestrial life. The section also mentions the trial of Galileo and the postulation of the theory of gravity by Isaac Newton.

19th Century ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the 19th century, highlighting the increased speculation about life on Mars following telescopic observations of apparent Martian canals. Percival Lowell is mentioned for his books proposing that the canals were the work of a long-gone civilization. William Wallace Campbell is mentioned for his spectroscopic analysis of Mars’s atmosphere. Louis Pasteur’s disproving of spontaneous generation is also discussed. Recent History ðŸ”—

This section discusses the emergence of astrobiology as a science. NASA, ESA, and INAF are mentioned as organizations that study astrobiology. The section also highlights the emergence of pseudosciences, such as ufology and ancient astronauts, due to public interest in extraterrestrial life. Lewis White Beck is mentioned for his efforts to elevate the level of public discourse on extraterrestrial life. The section concludes by discussing the technological advances and increased knowledge that have fueled the interest in extraterrestrial life in the 21st century.

Government Responses ðŸ”—

This section examines the government responses to the possibility of extraterrestrial life. The 1967 Outer Space Treaty and the 1979 Moon Agreement are mentioned for defining rules of planetary protection. COSPAR is also mentioned for providing guidelines for planetary protection. The United Nations Office for Outer Space Affairs’ discussions on strategies for interacting with extraterrestrial life are highlighted. The NASA Office of Safety and Mission Assurance (OSMA) is mentioned for its mission to prevent backward contamination of Earth by extraterrestrial life. The Chinese government’s space program and its objective to search for extraterrestrial life are also noted. Dmitry Rogozin, Isaac Ben-Israel, and Haim Eshed are mentioned for their views on extraterrestrial life.

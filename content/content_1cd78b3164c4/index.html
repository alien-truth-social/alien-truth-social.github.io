Reptilian Conspiracy Theory ðŸ”—

This article delves into the conspiracy theory surrounding reptilian humanoids, often referred to as reptoids, archons, reptiloids, saurians, draconians, or lizard people. It explores their role in fantasy, science fiction, ufology, and conspiracy theories. The central idea is that these reptilian beings are shapeshifting aliens who control Earth by infiltrating positions of power and manipulating human societies.

Origins ðŸ”—

This section traces the origins of the reptilian conspiracy theory, attributing its roots to the fiction of Robert E. Howard, the creator of Conan the Barbarian, specifically his story “The Shadow Kingdom” published in 1929. The story drew on theosophical ideas of lost worlds like Atlantis and Lemuria, particularly Helena Blavatsky’s “The Secret Doctrine,” which mentioned “dragon-men.” Howard’s serpent men were humanoids with snake heads who could imitate humans and control minds. Clark Ashton Smith also used these themes, contributing to the Cthulhu Mythos.

The article also mentions Maurice Doreal, an occultist, who wrote about a “serpent race” in his pamphlet “Mysteries of the Gobi” and poem “The Emerald Tablets.” Doreal’s ideas are believed to have influenced David Icke’s later work.

Historian Edward Guimont argues that the reptilian conspiracy theory, particularly as expounded by Icke, drew from earlier pseudohistorical legends developed during the colonisation of Africa, particularly surrounding Great Zimbabwe and the mokele-mbembe.

Key People:

  • Robert E. Howard: Author of “The Shadow Kingdom,” credited with originating the concept of serpent men.
  • Helena Blavatsky: Theosophist whose writings influenced Howard.
  • Clark Ashton Smith: Author who expanded on Howard’s serpent men concept.
  • Maurice Doreal: Occultist who wrote about a serpent race.
  • Edward Guimont: Historian who argues that the reptilian conspiracy theory drew from earlier pseudohistorical legends developed during the colonisation of Africa.

Alien Abduction ðŸ”—

This section discusses the connection between reptilian creatures and alien abduction narratives. It cites the case of Ashland, Nebraska police officer Herbert Schirmer, who claimed to have been abducted by reptilian-like beings in 1967. Schirmer’s claims are viewed skeptically.

Key People:

  • Herbert Schirmer: Police officer who claimed to have been abducted by reptilian beings.

Locations:

  • Ashland, Nebraska: Site of Herbert Schirmer’s alleged abduction.

David Icke ðŸ”—

This section focuses on David Icke, a British conspiracy theorist who popularized the reptilian conspiracy theory in his 1999 book “The Biggest Secret.” Icke claims that tall, blood-drinking, shapeshifting reptilian humanoids from the Alpha Draconis star system control the world from underground bases. He alleges that many world leaders, including the Merovingian dynasty, the Rothschilds, the Bush family, and the British Royal family, are related to these reptilians. Icke’s theories have gained a following in numerous countries.

A 2013 poll indicated that 4% of registered voters in the United States believed in David Icke’s ideas.

Key People:

  • David Icke: British conspiracy theorist who popularized the reptilian conspiracy theory.

Locations:

  • Alpha Draconis: Star system from which the reptilians are said to originate.

Politics ðŸ”—

This section examines instances where the reptilian conspiracy theory has intersected with politics. It mentions:

  • A 2003 Ontario, Canada, election campaign where an opponent was jokingly called an “evil reptilian kitten-eater from another planet.”
  • A 2008 U.S. Senate election in Minnesota where a ballot included a write-in vote for “Lizard People.”
  • A 2011 radio show appearance where comedian Louis C.K. jokingly asked Donald Rumsfeld if he and Dick Cheney were lizard people.
  • A 2013 Wired report about shapeshifting reptilian humanoids guarding Barack Obama, which led to a humorous response from the National Security Council.
  • In October 2022, Dutch MP Thierry Baudet, head of the far-right Forum for Democracy, said in an interview that he believes that the world is “being governed by evil reptiles.”

It also notes that some adherents of the QAnon conspiracy theory have incorporated elements of the reptilian conspiracy theory.

Key People:

  • Ernie Eves: Ontario politician who made a joke about a reptilian opponent.
  • Dalton McGuinty: Ontario politician targeted by the reptilian joke.
  • Al Franken: Comedian and politician who won a U.S. Senate election.
  • Norm Coleman: Incumbent Senator who lost to Al Franken.
  • Louis C.K.: Comedian who joked about Donald Rumsfeld being a lizard person.
  • Donald Rumsfeld: Former U.S. Secretary of Defense who was jokingly asked about being a lizard person.
  • Barack Obama: U.S. President whose security detail was jokingly linked to reptilians.
  • Thierry Baudet: Dutch MP who believes the world is governed by evil reptiles.

Locations:

  • Ontario, Canada: Site of the “evil reptilian kitten-eater” incident.
  • Minnesota: Site of the “Lizard People” write-in vote.

This section discusses terracotta figurines dating to 4500-5500 BC (belonging to the Ubaid period) that have been found in Ur, Mesopotamia. Some of these figurines depict “lizard heads” or “Ophidian figures.” Some conspiracy theorists cite these figurines as evidence for the “reptilian thesis.”

Locations:

  • Ur, Mesopotamia (present-day Iraq): Site where the reptilian figurines were found.

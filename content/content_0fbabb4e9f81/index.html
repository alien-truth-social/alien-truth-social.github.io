Cattle Mutilation ðŸ”—

Cattle mutilation refers to the killing and mutilation of cattle (and other animals) under unusual and often bloodless circumstances. The phenomenon is often linked to paranormal or supernatural explanations, including cults, government agencies, cryptids like the chupacabra, and extraterrestrials. Mutilations typically involve the removal of specific body parts such as ears, eyeballs, jaw flesh, tongues, lymph nodes, genitals, and rectums. Two independent federal investigations in the United States have been conducted into these incidents.

Early History ðŸ”—

Documented reports of unexplained livestock deaths date back to 1606 in England, with sheep being slaughtered and only tallow and inward parts removed. Charles Fort collected accounts of cattle mutilations in England during the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

The “Snippy” Horse Death of 1967 ðŸ”—

The case of “Snippy,” a horse found mutilated in Alamosa, Colorado, in 1967, brought widespread attention to unexplained livestock deaths. The horse’s head and neck were skinned and defleshed, with precise cuts and no blood at the scene. Agnes King and her son Harry discovered the body. The incident fueled speculation about extraterrestrial involvement. Wadsworth Ayer’s investigation for the Condon Committee found no evidence of abnormal causes. Sheriff Ben Phillips initially suggested a lightning strike. Later, two students confessed to shooting the horse weeks after the publicity.

This period saw waves of cattle mutilations reported in Kansas, Nebraska, Colorado, and New Mexico. In April 1973, sheriffs in Western Iowa reported unidentified helicopters involved in cattle and pig rustling. Similar rustling was reported in Missouri in August.

1973 Kansas Wave ðŸ”—

In June 1973, Cloud County sheriff Fred Modlin warned the public about a caller posing as a USDA official. A series of butchered cows were found on farms near Munden, Kansas, with right ears removed. Local press speculated about a “Mad Dog” or “phantom” Butcher. By December, law enforcement reported 40 mutilations across Kansas and Nebraska, with sexual organs removed. State Senator Ross Doyen reported a mutilation on his ranch. The Kansas Brand Commissioner’s office attributed most deaths to natural causes, a conclusion disputed by Modlin.

1974 Nebraska Wave ðŸ”—

Reports resumed in April 1974 with a mutilated and blood-drained cow in Nebraska. The North American Newspaper Alliance reported on the 1973 mutilations and helicopter sightings. In May, mutilations were reported in Mills County, Iowa, with coyotes suggested as the cause. By June, mutilations were reported in Lancaster County, Nebraska. Custer County reported helicopter sightings and armed patrols. In August, an unidentified helicopter was spotted hovering above a field where a mutilated cow was later found near Agnew, Nebraska. Knox County Sheriff Herbert Thompson reported armed civilian patrols. Cloud County Attorney William Walsh suggested devil-worshiping cultists were responsible. State Sen. John Decamp called for a coordinated investigation. By late August, 25 mutilations were reported across five Nebraska counties. In September, authorities ordered helicopters to fly above 1000 feet after a commercial helicopter was shot at. A meeting of law enforcement officials from 24 counties estimated 27 out of 80 cow deaths were suspected mutilations. South Dakota Attorney General Kermit Sande reported mutilations in his state. UFO expert J. Allen Hynek denied any link between UFOs and mutilations. In December 1974, mutilations were reported in Minnesota.

1975 Colorado ðŸ”—

In 1975, the Colorado Associated Press voted the mutilations as the state’s top story. Senator Floyd K. Haskell contacted the FBI, claiming 130 mutilations in Colorado and reports across nine states. A 1979 FBI report estimated 8,000 mutilations in Colorado, causing $1 million in damages. Donald E. Flickinger of the Michigan ATF investigated possible links to a “Hell-oriented biker gang.” The Colorado Bureau of Investigation opened an investigation under Carl Whiteside. The New York Times published a story examining cattle mutilations across 11 states.

1976 Dulce, New Mexico ðŸ”—

On June 13, 1976, Dulce rancher Manuel Gomez reported a mutilation to Officer Gabe Valdez of the New Mexico State Patrol.

1979 Public Meeting ðŸ”—

On April 20, 1979, U.S. Attorney R. E. Thompson and US Senator Harrison Schmidt held a public meeting about cattle mutilations. Paul Bennewitz reported seeing unidentified lights over Kirtland Air Force Base. In May 1979, Valdez reported finding tranquilizers and anticoagulants in a mutilated bull. In June 1981, investigators found a mutilated cow at the Gomez ranch with radar chaff nearby.

Later Incidents ðŸ”—

Many cases of mutilation have been reported worldwide since 1967, chiefly in the Americas and Australia. In South America, an estimated 3,500 incidents have occurred since 2002.

Characteristics ðŸ”—

Unusual cattle deaths are characterized by the absence of sex organs, udders, tongues, anus, rectum, and sometimes ears or eyes. Cuts are described as “clean” and “surgical.” Some animals are reported to have been drained of blood with no sign of blood in the immediate area. Howard Burgess stated that nearly 90 percent of mutilated cattle are between four and five years old. Strange marks or imprints near the site have been found. In the “Snippy” case, there was an absence of tracks and several small holes were found seemingly “punched” in the ground. In Rio Arriba County, New Mexico, a “trail of suction cup-like impressions” was found leading from a mutilated cow.

Laboratory Reports ðŸ”—

Laboratory reports on some mutilated animals have shown unusual levels of vitamins or minerals in tissue samples, and the presence of chemicals not normally found in animals. In a case documented by New Mexico police and the FBI involving a bull belonging to Manuel Gomez of Dulce, New Mexico, the animal displayed “classic” mutilation signs. The animal’s heart, bone, and muscle samples were sent to the Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory, while liver samples were sent to private laboratories. The liver was found to be completely devoid of copper and to contain 4 times the normal level of zinc, potassium, and phosphorus. Blood samples were reported to be “light pink in color” and “Did not clot after several days.”

Helicopter Sightings ðŸ”—

On July 15, 1974, two unregistered helicopters were reported to have opened fire on Robert Smith Jr. in Honey Creek, Iowa. On August 20, 1974, the Lincoln Journal Star reported that residents had seen unidentified helicopters shining spotlights into fields where mutilated cows were later found. By 1975, some ranchers formed armed vigilante groups. In 1976, two Cache County, Utah, police officers reportedly confronted men in an unmarked U.S. Army helicopter. On April 8, 1979, three police officers in Dulce, New Mexico, reported a mysterious aircraft resembling a U.S. military helicopter hovering around a site following a wave of alleged mutilation.

Official Investigations ðŸ”—

The New Mexico Livestock Board sought assistance from the Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory. Colorado Bureau of Investigation agents went undercover to investigate. Oklahoma convened a task force. Federal authorities launched a comprehensive investigation.

Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms ðŸ”—

In January 1975, the Minnesota field office of the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms (ATF) launched an investigation headed by Agent Donald Flickinger, tasked with investigating possible connections between cults and cattle mutilation. Flickinger recorded unusual incidents and circumstantial evidence but found insufficient evidence of cult involvement.

Colorado Bureau of Investigation ðŸ”—

In May 1975, the Colorado Bureau of Investigation opened an investigation under the leadership of Carl Whiteside. 19 animals underwent necropsies at Colorado State University but no conclusive results emerged. The US Bureau of Land Management was forced to ground all helicopters in Eastern Colorado. By December, the CBI had investigated 203 reports of cattle mutilations.

New Mexico State Police ðŸ”—

On June 13, 1976, the New Mexico State Police began an investigation headed by Officer Gabe Valdez with the assistance of Cattle Inspector Jim Dyad and Officer Howard Johnston of the New Mexico Department of Game and Fish. On the night of July 5, Burgess and Valdez screened the Gomez herd; five animals were found to have been marked with a chemical that fluoresced under ultraviolet light. On June 14, 1981, intact radar chaff was found near a mutilated cow on the Gomez ranch. This investigation reported finding evidence that some mutilated animals had been tranquilized and treated with an anti-coagulant prior to their mutilation.

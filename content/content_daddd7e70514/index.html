David Grusch: Former U.S. Military Intelligence Officer’s Claims About “Non-Human” Spacecraft Recovery ðŸ”—

This article focuses on the claims made by David Grusch, a former U.S. Air Force officer and intelligence official, regarding the U.S. government’s alleged involvement in recovering and reverse-engineering “non-human” spacecraft. It also covers the responses to these claims from various experts and government entities.

Background ðŸ”—

David Charles Grusch is introduced as an Afghanistan combat veteran and former intelligence officer who worked at the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA) and the National Reconnaissance Office (NRO). From 2019 to 2021, he served as the NRO representative to the Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force. He assisted in drafting the National Defense Authorization Act of 2023, which included whistleblower protections for UFO reporting. The section notes that Congressional interest in UFOs increased prior to Grusch’s claims due to the downing of four objects by the Air Force in February 2023.

Grusch’s Claims ðŸ”—

This section details Grusch’s allegations, which were initially reported by Leslie Kean and Ralph Blumenthal in The Debrief. Grusch claims the U.S. government possesses multiple spacecraft of “non-human” origin and the corpses of their pilots. He alleges a secret UFO retrieval program, “substantive evidence” of white-collar crime to conceal UFO programs, and that officials have stated people have been killed to conceal these programs.

In an interview with Le Parisien, Grusch elaborated, suggesting UFOs could be from extra dimensions, mentioning “football-field” sized crafts, the transfer of crashed UFOs to defense contractors, and “malevolent activity” by UFOs.

During a Congressional hearing in July 2023, Grusch testified about being informed of a multi-decade UAP crash retrieval and reverse-engineering program to which he was denied access. He stated his belief that the U.S. government possesses UAP based on interviews with 40 witnesses. He also claimed the U.S. has retrieved “non-human ‘biologics’” from the crafts. When asked about harm or injury related to cover-ups, Grusch said yes, but could only provide details in a SCIF.

Responses from Relevant Experts ðŸ”—

This section presents the views of various experts on Grusch’s claims. It highlights that his assertions are based on alleged documents and conversations, rather than testable evidence. The scientific community generally considers claims of government conspiracy to conceal extraterrestrial visitation untrue.

Joshua Semeter of NASA’s UAP independent study team states that without data or material evidence, evaluating the claims is impossible. Adam Frank, an astrophysics professor, criticizes the claims as hearsay and lacking extraordinary evidence.

Stuart Clark’s opinion piece in The Guardian includes questions from scientists like Avi Loeb, Michael Garrett, and Sara Russell, who point out the lack of observed extraterrestrial activity and the ease of identifying extraterrestrial materials.

Greg Eghigian, a history professor and UFO expert, notes the historical pattern of individuals making “bombshell allegations” about UFOs and the lack of corroboration. Seth Shostak, senior astronomer at the SETI Institute, questions the absence of physical evidence and dismisses the claims as an “argument from authority.” Michael Shermer, publisher of Skeptic magazine, notes the lack of physical evidence and scientific community involvement.

Physicist Sean M. Carroll calls Grusch’s claims “laughable” and describes him as a “complete crackpot.” NASA’s JPL director, Laurie Leshin, denies having seen spacecraft from outside this world. Michio Kaku suggests the burden of proof has shifted to the Pentagon, a statement criticized by Ross Pomeroy.

Responses from the United States Government ðŸ”—

This section outlines the responses from various U.S. government entities.

Department of Defense and NASA ðŸ”—

White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre referred questions to the Department of Defense (DoD). A Pentagon spokesperson stated that AARO has not discovered verifiable information to substantiate claims of programs regarding the possession or reverse-engineering of extraterrestrial materials. General Mark Milley stated he had never encountered evidence verifying Grusch’s claims. NASA stated it has not found credible evidence of extraterrestrial life and that there is no evidence that UAPs are extraterrestrial.

Congress ðŸ”—

Representative Mike Turner stated there is no evidence of the U.S. possessing UFOs from outer space. Senator Lindsey Graham found the claims unreasonable. Senator Josh Hawley said the allegations sounded close to what they were “grudgingly admitted to us in the briefing”. Senator Kirsten Gillibrand intends to hold a hearing to assess whether “rogue SAP programs” existed. Senator Marco Rubio said people with “first-hand knowledge” have shared information with his committee.

In July 2023, Senators Chuck Schumer and Mike Rounds proposed the UAP Disclosure Act 2023, an amendment to the 2024 National Defence Authorization Act, to provide wider access to UAP records. The enrolled bill directs the National Archives to collect government documents about UAP.

In January 2024, members of the House Oversight Committee received a classified briefing from the Intelligence Community Inspector General (IC IG) Thomas A. Monheim regarding UAP reporting transparency. Some members were frustrated by the lack of new information regarding Grusch’s allegations.

2023 House Committee Oversight and Accountability Hearing ðŸ”—

Representatives Anna Paulina Luna and Tim Burchett organized a hearing on July 26, 2023, in response to Grusch’s claims. Grusch testified, repeating his earlier UFO claims. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez asked where to look for evidence, and Grusch offered to provide specifics in a closed environment. Officials later informed lawmakers that Grusch lacked security clearance to discuss the issues in a SCIF. Following the hearing, a bipartisan group of U.S. representatives called for the formation of a select committee on UAPs with subpoena power.

AARO’s director Sean Kirkpatrick wrote that Grusch “has refused to speak with AARO” and that the hearing was “insulting”.

Sean Kirkpatrick ðŸ”—

After retiring from AARO, Kirkpatrick wrote an opinion piece for Scientific American stating that the US Government UFO coverup allegations “derive from inadvertent or unauthorized disclosures of legitimate U.S. programs or related R&D that have nothing to do with extraterrestrial issues or technology.” He described the allegations’ supporters as “a small group of interconnected believers and others with possibly less than honest intentions,” who promote a “whirlwind of tall tales, fabrication and secondhand or thirdhand retellings”.

In an interview with Peter Bergen, Kirkpatrick said that Grusch may have misinterpreted things or fallen under the influence of a core group of people. He characterized the situation as a “self licking ice cream cone,” where UFO true believers are carrying out a conspiracy to get the government involved in investigating aliens.

Media Reporting on Grusch’s Claims ðŸ”—

This section examines how the media has covered Grusch’s claims.

Connections to Studies Funded by Robert Bigelow ðŸ”—

Keith Kloor draws a connection between Grusch’s claims and studies funded by Robert Bigelow, noting specific references to “a football fieldâ€“sized UFO” in both.

Reporting on Psychiatric Treatment Received by Grusch ðŸ”—

Ken Klippenstein reported in The Intercept that Grusch was twice committed after incidents involving drunkenness and suicidal comments. Police records mentioned PTSD. Grusch was placed under an emergency custody order and transported to an emergency room. Grusch sued Loudoun County Sheriff Mike Chapman for releasing the information.

News Stories and Commentary ðŸ”—

Nick Pope, who previously ran the British Ministry of Defense “UFO Desk”, expressed doubt about confirming Grusch’s claims. Marina Koren wrote that the case fits a pattern of unprovable claims. Matt Laslo described the sympathetic hearing of Grusch’s claims by some members of Congress as an indication that “once-fringe notions have built-in fan bases in today’s Capitol”. Tucker Carlson publicized the claims. Tom Rogan was skeptical but opined that they should be further investigated.

Andrew Prokop wrote that skeptics question whether Grusch is repeating tall tales. Sean Thomas expressed confusion that others have tried to convince officials and the public that UFOs are worthy of serious considerations. Ross Douthat noted that one interpretation is that parts of the U.S. government see benefit in promoting belief in UFOs. Matt Stieb described Grusch’s claims as “crazy”.

Ezra Klein interviewed Leslie Kean, noting that reactions to her story about Grusch have been either to embrace it as definitive truth or dismiss it out of hand. Steven Greenstreet criticized Grusch for previously attending UFO conventions and associating with Jeremy Corbell and George Knapp.

The story received attention from multiple foreign mainstream news outlets.

Disinformation Campaign Allegations ðŸ”—

Grusch claimed that intentional disinformation was being promoted by the US government to cast doubt on the veracity of “non-human” UFO claims. Adam Gabbatt described Grusch’s position as “a common conspiracy trope in the UFO community”. Others have suggested that Grusch was given disinformation about aliens. Gareth Nicholson wrote that “the current UAP flap could be an attempt by the US military to engage in a disinformation campaign to disguise real aerospace breakthroughs or an attempt to flush out advanced technologies held by rivals such as Russia and China”.

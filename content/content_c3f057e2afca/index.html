Washington, D.C. UFO Incident ðŸ”—

This article details the series of unidentified flying object (UFO) sightings that occurred in Washington, D.C., during July 1952. These sightings, known as the “Washington flap,” “Washington National Airport Sightings,” or the “Invasion of Washington,” garnered significant media attention and became one of the most well-known UFO incidents.

1952 UFO flap ðŸ”—

This section provides context for the Washington D.C. sightings by describing the broader wave of UFO reports that occurred in the summer of 1952. It highlights the unprecedented media attention and the large number of UFO reports received by the US Air Force. Key figures mentioned include Edward J. Ruppelt, the director of Project Blue Book, who noted the extensive media coverage. The section also mentions events leading up to the Washington sightings, such as an explosion in Seattle, an Associated Press report on the Air Force’s interest in UFOs, and articles in Life and Look magazines discussing the phenomenon.

Sightings over Washington, D.C. ðŸ”—

This section details the specific UFO sightings that occurred over Washington, D.C., on two consecutive Saturday nights in July 1952.

Night of July 19 ðŸ”—

This subsection describes the initial sightings on July 19, 1952. Edward Nugent, an air traffic controller at Washington National Airport, first spotted seven objects on radar. Harry Barnes, a senior air-traffic controller, confirmed the unusual movements of the objects. Controllers at the airport’s control tower, Howard Cocklin and Joe Zacko, also observed the radar blips and a “bright light” in the sky. Airman William Brady at Andrews Air Force Base reported seeing an “orange ball of fire.” S.C. Pierman, a Capital Airlines pilot, observed six “white, tailless, fast-moving lights.” The section also mentions radar tracking of an object hovering over a radio beacon. The arrival of two Air Force F-94 Starfire jets from New Castle Air Force Base temporarily dispersed the objects.

Publicity and Air Force reaction ðŸ”—

This subsection discusses the widespread media coverage of the July 19-20 sightings. It mentions that USAF Captain Edward J. Ruppelt, supervisor of Project Blue Book, was in Washington at the time but faced difficulties in investigating the sightings. The section also notes that an Air Force radar specialist, Captain Roy James, suggested that unusual weather conditions could have caused the radar targets.

Night of July 26 ðŸ”—

This subsection details the events of July 26, 1952. A pilot and stewardess on a National Airlines flight reported seeing lights above their plane. Radar centers at National Airport and Andrews AFB tracked more unknown objects. USAF master sergeant Charles E. Cummings visually observed the objects at Andrews. Albert M. Chop, the press spokesman for Project Blue Book, arrived at National Airport. Two F-94 Starfire jets were scrambled, with Lieutenant William Patterson reporting seeing four white “glows.” Major Dewey Fournet, Project Blue Book’s liaison at the Pentagon, and Lt. John Holcomb, a United States Navy radar specialist, arrived at National Airport. Civilian aircraft also reported glowing objects.

Aftermath ðŸ”—

This section briefly mentions a report from August 19, where West Palm Beach scoutmaster Sonny DesVergers claimed to have been burned by a flying disc.

Air Force explanation ðŸ”—

This section describes the Air Force’s official explanation for the sightings. Major Generals John Samford and Roger M. Ramey held a press conference at the Pentagon on July 29, 1952, stating that the visual sightings could be explained as misidentified aerial phenomena and the radar targets by temperature inversion. The crew of a B-25 bomber and Air Force Captain Harold May provided accounts supporting the Air Force’s explanation. The CAA’s Technical Development and Evaluation Center also concluded that a temperature inversion was present during the sightings. Donald Menzel and Philip Klass are mentioned as prominent UFO skeptics who supported the temperature inversion/mirage hypothesis.

Criticisms ðŸ”—

This subsection presents criticisms of the Air Force’s explanation. Edward J. Ruppelt, radar and control tower personnel, and some Air Force officers disagreed with the official explanation. Michael Wertheimer, a researcher for the Condon Report, stated that radar witnesses still disputed the Air Force explanation. Former radar controller Howard Cocklin maintained that he saw an object on the radar screen and out the window.

White House concern and CIA interest ðŸ”—

This section discusses the concern of President Harry Truman and the CIA regarding the UFO sightings. Truman requested an explanation from Ruppelt. CIA historian Gerald Haines noted Truman’s concern and the CIA’s formation of a special study group to review the situation. Edward Tauss reported for the group that most UFO sightings could be easily explained, but recommended continued monitoring.

The Robertson Panel ðŸ”—

This section describes the creation of the Robertson Panel in January 1953 due to the high number of UFO reports. The CIA and Air Force were concerned about potential mass panic and enemy exploitation. Howard P. Robertson chaired the panel, which consisted of prominent scientists. The panel dismissed most UFO cases and recommended that the Air Force and Project Blue Book focus on debunking UFO reports.

In popular culture ðŸ”—

This section notes the appearances of the 1952 Washington, D.C. UFO incident in popular culture, specifically in the History Channel television series Project Blue Book and the Netflix series Top Secret UFO Projects Declassified.

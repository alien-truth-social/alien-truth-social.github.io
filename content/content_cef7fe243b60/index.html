This article provides an overview of the history of UFO investigations conducted by the United States government, from the late 1940s to the present day. It notes that despite decades of investigation, no evidence of UFOs with extraordinary origins has been found, attributing reported sightings to natural phenomena, human technology, misinterpretations, delusions, or hoaxes.

Investigation and analysis of reported UFO incidents under the federal government of the United States ðŸ”—

This section serves as an introduction to the topic, stating that the US government has been investigating UFOs since 1947 through various branches and agencies. It emphasizes that despite decades of interest, no evidence supports the extraordinary origins of UFOs, attributing sightings to natural phenomena, human technology, misapprehensions, delusions, or hoaxes. It mentions the initial Air Force projects (Sign, Grudge, Blue Book) and the Condon Report’s conclusion that further UFO study was not justified. It also highlights the 2017 New York Times report on the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) and the Pentagon’s release of UAP videos. Finally, it notes the establishment of the Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force (UAPTF), congressional hearings on UAPs, NASA’s UAP study team, and the creation of the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO).

U.S. government activity from 1947 to 1969 ðŸ”—

This section focuses on the early period of US government UFO investigations, primarily through Projects Sign, Grudge, and Blue Book.

Projects Sign, Grudge, and Blue Book ðŸ”—

This section details the three main Air Force projects dedicated to investigating UFOs during the specified time frame. It mentions Steven J. Dick’s analysis of the Air Force’s role in officially recognizing the extraterrestrial hypothesis. The section discusses the Mantell incident and the challenges the Air Force faced in quashing extraterrestrial rumors. It describes Project Sign’s initial extraterrestrial hypothesis, which was rejected by General Hoyt S. Vandenburg. It contrasts Project Sign with Project Grudge, which adopted a more skeptical approach. The section also highlights the role of J. Allen Hynek, who advocated for continued UFO studies. It provides statistics on the number of reported sightings investigated under these projects and the percentage that remained unidentified. It also mentions the CIA’s involvement and the Robertson Panel’s concerns about the sensationalization of UFO reports. Finally, it touches on Air Force Regulation 200-2, which governed the release of information about UFOs to the media.

Condon Report and Congressional hearings ðŸ”—

This section discusses the Condon Report’s conclusion that further UFO study was not justified and mentions the Congressional hearings held on the topic in 1966 and 1969.

Overview of government activity from 1947 to 1969 ðŸ”—

This section provides a summary of government activity during this period. It notes that most military and defense authorities concluded that UFOs posed no threat to national security, but investigations were shrouded in secrecy due to classified aviation projects. It contrasts the US approach with that of other governments, which have released formerly classified UFO documents. It also mentions J. Allen Hynek and Nick Pope as exceptions to the consensus against government investment in UFO investigations. Finally, it cites Robert Sheaffer’s assessment that Project Blue Book did not yield any significant findings.

U.S. government inactivity from 1969 to 2017 ðŸ”—

This section covers the period of nearly five decades when the US government did not have public programs for investigating UFOs. It mentions a 1977 request from Frank Press to NASA to reopen investigations, and NASA’s initial inclination to agree, followed by a decision against establishing a research activity or convening a symposium on the subject. It also notes the Air Force’s release of two investigative reports on the Roswell incident in the 1990s due to renewed media interest.

U.S. government activity from 2017 to present ðŸ”—

This section details the renewed interest and activity by the US government in UFO investigations starting in 2017.

Formation of UAPTF and Pentagon release of UAP videos ðŸ”—

This section discusses the 2017 New York Times report revealing the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP) and the subsequent change in the conversation around UFOs. It mentions the Pentagon’s release of UAP videos and the establishment of the Unidentified Aerial Phenomena Task Force (UAPTF). It also notes Barack Obama’s statement about unexplained objects in the skies and the release of the “Preliminary Assessment: Unidentified Aerial Phenomena” report. It describes the May 17, 2022, congressional hearings on UAPs, where Pentagon officials reported a database of 400 UAP incidents.

Formation of NASA’s UAP study team and the Pentagon’s AARO ðŸ”—

This section covers the establishment of NASA’s UAP independent study team and the replacement of the UAPTF with the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO). It mentions Sean Kirkpatrick’s testimony before the Senate Armed Services Committee and Avi Loeb’s critique of the testimony. It notes the first public meeting of the NASA team on May 31, 2023, and AARO’s ongoing investigation of over 800 UAP reports. It also discusses the July 26, 2023, Congressional hearing featuring testimony from David Grusch, Ryan Graves, and David Fravor, including Grusch’s claims about a multi-decade UAP crash retrieval and reverse engineering program and the retrieval of “non-human” biological matter. Finally, it mentions the strengthening of UAP reporting requirements and the proposed legislation to create a commission for declassifying government UFO documents.

UAP Disclosure Act ðŸ”—

This section describes the Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena Disclosure Act (UAPDA), a series of bipartisan bills aimed at compelling the disclosure of UFO/UAP data, biological materials, and technologies originating from non-human extraterrestrial intelligence held by the US government and its contractors. It notes that the UAPDA was introduced as an amendment to the National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2024.

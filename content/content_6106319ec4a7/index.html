UFO (British TV series) ðŸ”—

A British science fiction television series that aired from 1970 to 1971, focusing on Earth’s defense against an alien invasion in the then-future of 1980.

Premise ðŸ”—

In 1980, Earth is under threat from aliens from a dying planet who are abducting humans for their organs. SHADO (Supreme Headquarters Alien Defence Organisation), a secret international military agency operating under the guise of a movie studio, is formed to defend Earth. SHADO utilizes advanced technology, including a space intruder detection satellite (SID), lunar interceptors, a submarine/aircraft called Skydiver, and ground-based SHADO Mobiles. The aliens are humanoid, requiring a green oxygenated liquid and special contact lenses. Their UFOs can travel faster than light but have limited time in Earth’s atmosphere.

Cast and characters ðŸ”—

The series features a large ensemble cast with frequent changes. Ed Bishop as Commander Ed Straker is the only actor to appear in every episode.

Main characters ðŸ”—

  • Commander Edward Straker (Ed Bishop): The head of SHADO, posing as the head of Harlington-Straker Film Studios.
  • Colonel Paul Foster (Michael Billington): A former test pilot recruited into SHADO.
  • Lieutenant Gay Ellis (Gabrielle Drake): Moonbase commander.
  • Colonel Alec Freeman (George Sewell): SHADO’s second-in-command.
  • General James Henderson (Grant Taylor): Straker’s superior and president of the International Astrophysical Commission.
  • Colonel Virginia Lake (Wanda Ventham): A SHADO operative who later becomes first officer.
  • Captain Peter Carlin (Peter Gordeno): First commander of Skydiver.
  • Lieutenant Nina Barry (Dolores Mantez): Moonbase operative.
  • Captain Lew Waterman (Gary Myers): Second commander of Skydiver.
  • Lieutenant Keith Ford (Keith Alexander): Communications officer.
  • Lieutenant Ayshea Johnson (Ayshea Brough): SHADO headquarters officer.
  • Doctor Douglas Jackson (Vladek Sheybal): SHADO psychiatrist and science officer.
  • Lieutenant Joan Harrington (Antonia Ellis): Moonbase operative.
  • Miss Ealand (Norma Ronald): Straker’s secretary.
  • Lieutenant Mark Bradley (Harry Baird): Interceptor pilot.

Minor characters ðŸ”—

Shakira Baksh (later Michael Caine’s wife) played Joanna, a Moonbase operative. Steven Berkoff played Interceptor pilot Steve Minto, and Georgina Moon played Lieutenant Sylvia Howell.

Episodes ðŸ”—

The 26 episodes were broadcast in a non-chronological order by ITV. The list provides the original ATV (Midlands) broadcast order, along with director, writer, original air date, and production code. Episode titles include “Identified,” “Exposed,” “The Cat with Ten Lives,” “Conflict,” “A Question of Priorities,” and “The Long Sleep.”

Different viewing orders exist, including production order, ATV broadcast order, and recommended orders by UFO Series, Fanderson, and ITC. A table compares these different orders.

Production ðŸ”—

Lew Grade approached Gerry Anderson to create a live-action TV series, resulting in UFO. The series was influenced by UFO sightings and Christiaan Barnard’s transplant operations. Sylvia Anderson designed the costumes, including the purple wigs for female Moonbase staff. The Harlington-Straker Studio was the actual studio where the series was filmed.

Filming ðŸ”—

Filming began in April 1969 at MGM-British Studios, then moved to Pinewood Studios in June 1970. George Sewell and Gabrielle Drake left after the studio change. Harry Baird and Peter Gordeno also left the series.

Costumes ðŸ”—

Female Moonbase personnel wore purple wigs, silver catsuits, and heavy eye makeup for a futuristic look. Ed Bishop initially bleached his hair, then wore a white wig. Other male characters also wore wigs. Only two alien suits were made.

Special effects ðŸ”—

Derek Meddings supervised the special effects, using techniques like double-walled visors and underwater effects.

Vehicle design ðŸ”—

Derek Meddings and Mike Trim designed the vehicles. The gull-winged cars were originally built for DoppelgÃ¤nger. The blue SHADO Jeeps were built on Mini Moke chassis. Few original props and miniatures have survived.

Broadcast ðŸ”—

Broadcasters were unsure if UFO was for adults or children. In the UK, episodes were shown in the late afternoon and Saturday mornings.

Reception ðŸ”—

Reviews of UFO are mixed. Some praise the visuals, music, and costumes, while others criticize the writing and pacing. Some reviews note the series’ mix of adult themes and science fiction elements.

Aborted second series ðŸ”—

ITC commissioned a second series, UFO 2, set entirely on the Moon. However, when ratings dropped, ITC cancelled the plans. Gerry Anderson then created Space: 1999 from the pre-production work.

Merchandise ðŸ”—

The series generated a range of toys, including Dinky die-cast vehicles.

Compilation films ðŸ”—

Several episodes were compiled into films, including Invasion: UFO. Italian producers also created compilation films with mixed reviews.

Home media ðŸ”—

Channel 5 and ITC released VHS collections. The complete series was released on DVD in 2002.

Proposed feature film ðŸ”—

In 2009, a feature film adaptation was announced, but it did not enter production.

In other media ðŸ”—

Stories set in the UFO universe have appeared in novels, comics, and video games. Julian Gollop cited UFO as an influence for UFO: Enemy Unknown. Yoshiyuki Sadamoto drew inspiration from UFO for character designs in Neon Genesis Evangelion.

Translations ðŸ”—

The series has been translated into French, German, Japanese, Italian, Spanish, and Brazilian Portuguese.

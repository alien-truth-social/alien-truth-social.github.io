The Kepler space telescope, a NASA mission from 2009 to 2018, was designed to discover Earth-sized exoplanets. Named after astronomer Johannes Kepler, it operated in a heliocentric orbit. The mission ended when its reaction control system fuel was depleted.

History ðŸ”—

Pre-launch development ðŸ”—

The Kepler space telescope was part of NASA’s Discovery Program. The Jet Propulsion Laboratory managed construction and initial operation, while Ball Aerospace developed the flight system. Budget cuts and consolidation at NASA delayed the launch. The high-gain antenna design was changed to reduce cost and complexity.

Post launch ðŸ”—

The Ames Research Center managed ground system development, mission operations, and data analysis. The initial mission lifetime was 3.5 years, but noise in the data required more time. Reaction wheel failures in 2012 and 2013 threatened the mission. NASA sought alternative mission plans, leading to the K2 “Second Light” proposal. By 2015, Kepler had found over 1,000 confirmed exoplanets and thousands of candidates. Astronomers estimated billions of rocky, Earth-sized exoplanets in habitable zones. NASA announced the 1,000th confirmed exoplanet in 2015. In 2016, 1,284 new exoplanets were verified. Kepler data also helped study supernovae. The mission ended on October 30, 2018, after running out of fuel.

Spacecraft design ðŸ”—

The Kepler telescope had a mass of 1,039 kg and used a Schmidt camera with a 0.95-meter corrector plate and a 1.4-meter primary mirror. It had a 115 deg2 field of view.

Camera ðŸ”—

The camera’s focal plane consisted of forty-two 50 Ã— 25 mm CCDs with a total resolution of 94.6 megapixels. The CCDs were read out every 6.5 seconds and co-added on board. Data from selected pixels was requantized, compressed, and stored in a 16 gigabyte solid-state recorder.

Primary mirror ðŸ”—

The primary mirror was 1.4 meters in diameter, made by Corning using ultra-low expansion (ULE) glass. It had a nine-layer silver coating to enhance reflection.

Photometric performance ðŸ”—

Kepler’s photometric performance was good but fell short of design goals. The objective was 20 ppm CDPP for a magnitude 12 star for a 6.5-hour integration. The median obtained accuracy was 29 ppm.

Orbit and orientation ðŸ”—

Kepler orbits the Sun in an Earth-trailing orbit with a period of 372.5 days. The photometer pointed to a field in the constellations Cygnus, Lyra, and Draco. Orientation was three-axis stabilized using fine-guidance sensors and reaction wheels.

Operations ðŸ”—

The Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics (LASP) operated Kepler from Boulder, Colorado. The spacecraft’s solar array was rotated at solstices and equinoxes.

Communications ðŸ”—

NASA contacted Kepler using the X band communication link. Scientific data was downloaded monthly using the Ka band link.

Data management ðŸ”—

Science data was processed by the Kepler Data Management Center (DMC) at the Space Telescope Science Institute and the Science Operations Center (SOC) at NASA Ames Research Center. Calibrated data products and scientific results were archived and distributed to astronomers.

Reaction wheel failures ðŸ”—

Reaction wheel failures in 2012 and 2013 were traced to pitting caused by arcing between steel ball bearings, triggered by coronal mass ejections (CMEs).

Operational timeline ðŸ”—

The Kepler observatory launched on March 7, 2009, aboard a Delta II rocket from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station. The telescope cover was jettisoned on April 7, 2009. The focus was optimized in April 2009. Kepler began its search for planets on May 13, 2009. The first science data was sent to Earth on June 19, 2009. Safe mode events occurred in June and July 2009 due to a processor reset. A low voltage power supply was identified as the cause. In January 2010, a problem occurred with focal plane MOD-3 module.

Field of view ðŸ”—

Kepler had a fixed field of view (FOV) in the constellations Cygnus, Lyra, and Draco, covering 115 square degrees.

Objectives and methods ðŸ”—

Kepler’s objective was to explore the structure and diversity of planetary systems. It aimed to determine the number of Earth-size planets in or near habitable zones, the range of orbit sizes and shapes, the number of planets in multiple-star systems, and the properties of stars with planetary systems. The transit method was used to detect planets.

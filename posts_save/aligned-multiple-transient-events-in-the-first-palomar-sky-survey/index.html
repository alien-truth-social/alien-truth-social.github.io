Aligned, Multiple-transient Events in the First Palomar Sky Survey

Apr 30, 2026 1 minute read

In October 2025, astronomer Beatriz Villarroel and psychologist Stephen Bruehl published two companion papers in Publications of the Astronomical Society of the Pacific and Scientific Reports suggesting that transient light events in 1950s Palomar Observatory photographic plates could be linked to nuclear testing or unidentified anomalous phenomena. The research faced widespread skepticism from the scientific community, with experts such as Michael Wiescher and Nigel Hambly suggesting that the anomalies were likely due to plate defects, meteors, or other prosaic environmental factors. A formal rejoinder by physicists Kevin Knuth, Wesley Andres Watters, and Laura Dominé further challenged the authors’ methodology, citing issues with data preparation and a lack of microscopic analysis. While some independent analysis by researchers like Ivo Busko has noted similar transients in other archival datasets, the original papers were notably rejected by the preprint server arXiv for lacking sufficient scholarly rigor.
